# EEGManyLabs Replication Studies: Task-Concept-Event Analysis

## Overview

The #EEGManyLabs project (Pavlov et al., 2021, *Cortex*, 144, 213–229) is a large-scale, international multi-laboratory collaboration to assess the replicability of influential EEG/ERP experiments. After a systematic selection process, 20 studies were chosen for direct replication across a minimum of 3 independent laboratories using Registered Report protocols. The project website (eegmanylabs.org/replications/) now lists 24 replication studies.

This document analyzes each EEGManyLabs study in the same format as the companion task-concept documents, connecting each to the behavioral task paradigm, the target ERP/EEG component, cognitive concepts, and key references.

**Source:** [EEGManyLabs Replications](https://eegmanylabs.org/replications/) | [Pavlov et al. (2021)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cortex.2021.03.013)

### EML-1: Kutas & Hillyard (1980) — N400 and Semantic Processing

**Original Study:** Kutas, M., & Hillyard, S. A. (1980). Reading senseless sentences: Brain potentials reflect semantic incongruity. *Science*, 207(4427), 203–205.

**Task Paradigm:** Sentence Reading / Semantic Violation Detection. Participants read sentences word by word. The final word is either semantically congruent ("He spread the warm bread with *butter*") or incongruent ("He spread the warm bread with *socks*"). EEG is recorded time-locked to the final word.

**Target ERP Component:** **N400** — a negative deflection peaking ~400 ms post-stimulus over centroparietal sites, larger for semantically incongruent words.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Semantic processing and expectation
- Lexical-semantic integration
- Prediction and prediction error in language
- Contextual constraint effects

**Key Finding:** Semantically incongruent sentence endings elicit a large N400 compared to congruent endings, demonstrating that the brain rapidly detects and processes semantic violations during reading.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 29 (Sentence Comprehension / Garden-Path Reading)** in the task catalog. Also related to **Task 27 (Semantic Priming)** and **Task 26 (Lexical Decision)**.

**Key References:**
1. Kutas, M., & Hillyard, S. A. (1980). Reading senseless sentences: Brain potentials reflect semantic incongruity. *Science*, 207(4427), 203–205.
2. Kutas, M., & Federmeier, K. D. (2011). Thirty years and counting: Finding meaning in the N400 component of the event-related brain potential (ERP). *Annual Review of Psychology*, 62, 621–647.
3. Lau, E. F., Phillips, C., & Poeppel, D. (2008). A cortical network for semantics: (de)constructing the N400. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 9(12), 920–933.

### EML-2: Clark & Hillyard (1996) — Spatial Attention and Sensory Gain (N1)

**Original Study:** Clark, V. P., & Hillyard, S. A. (1996). Spatial selective attention affects early extrastriate but not striate components of the visual evoked potential. *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*, 8(5), 387–402.

**Task Paradigm:** Spatial Attention / Posner-Style Cueing. Participants maintain central fixation while stimuli are flashed at attended vs. unattended peripheral locations. EEG is recorded to the flashed stimuli.

**Target ERP Component:** **N1 (N150–200)** — enhanced amplitude for stimuli at attended vs. unattended locations, reflecting sensory gain control in extrastriate visual cortex.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Spatial selective attention
- Sensory gain modulation
- Early visual processing
- Striate vs. extrastriate cortex contributions

**Key Finding:** Spatial attention modulates the N1 component arising from extrastriate cortex but does not affect the earlier C1 component from striate cortex, supporting a sensory gain control model of attention operating at the level of V2/V3.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 3 (Posner Spatial Cueing Task)**.

**Key References:**
1. Clark, V. P., & Hillyard, S. A. (1996). Spatial selective attention affects early extrastriate but not striate components of the visual evoked potential. *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*, 8(5), 387–402.
2. Hillyard, S. A., & Anllo-Vento, L. (1998). Event-related brain potentials in the study of visual selective attention. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 95(3), 781–787.
3. Luck, S. J., Woodman, G. F., & Vogel, E. K. (2000). Event-related potential studies of attention. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 4(11), 432–440.

### EML-3: Eimer (1996) — N2pc and Attentional Selection

**Original Study:** Eimer, M. (1996). The N2pc component as an indicator of attentional selectivity. *Electroencephalography and Clinical Neurophysiology*, 99(3), 225–234.

**Task Paradigm:** Visual Search / Lateralized Attention. Participants search displays for a target defined by color or shape. Targets and distractors appear in left and right visual fields. EEG is recorded from posterior lateral electrode sites.

**Target ERP Component:** **N2pc** — a posterior contralateral negativity (200–300 ms) at electrode sites contralateral to the attended/target item, reflecting the spatial focusing of attention.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Attentional selection in visual search
- Spatial filtering of distractors
- Target localization
- Hemispheric lateralization of attention

**Key Finding:** The N2pc is enhanced contralateral to the target in visual search displays, providing a neural marker of the spatial allocation of attention to target items.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 4 (Visual Search Task)** and **Task 50 (Attention Network Test)**.

**Key References:**
1. Eimer, M. (1996). The N2pc component as an indicator of attentional selectivity. *Electroencephalography and Clinical Neurophysiology*, 99(3), 225–234.
2. Luck, S. J., & Hillyard, S. A. (1994). Spatial filtering during visual search: Evidence from human electrophysiology. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 20(5), 1000–1014.
3. Woodman, G. F., & Luck, S. J. (1999). Electrophysiological measurement of rapid shifts of attention during visual search. *Nature*, 400(6747), 867–869.

### EML-4: Luck, Vogel, & Shapiro (1996) — Attentional Blink and P3

**Original Study:** Luck, S. J., Vogel, E. K., & Shapiro, K. L. (1996). Word meanings can be accessed but not reported during the attentional blink. *Nature*, 383(6601), 616–618.

**Task Paradigm:** Attentional Blink (RSVP). Participants identify two targets (T1, T2) embedded in a rapid serial visual presentation stream. T2 appears at variable lags after T1. ERP is recorded to T2 items.

**Target ERP Component:** **P3 (P3b)** — suppressed for missed T2 items during the attentional blink window, while earlier sensory components (N400) remain intact, indicating that semantic processing occurs but conscious consolidation fails.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Temporal attention
- Working memory consolidation bottleneck
- Conscious access vs. unconscious processing
- Attentional capacity limits

**Key Finding:** During the attentional blink, T2 words still elicit an N400 to semantic violations, but the P3b is suppressed, demonstrating that meaning is extracted even when conscious report fails.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 5 (Attentional Blink Task)**.

**Key References:**
1. Luck, S. J., Vogel, E. K., & Shapiro, K. L. (1996). Word meanings can be accessed but not reported during the attentional blink. *Nature*, 383(6601), 616–618.
2. Vogel, E. K., Luck, S. J., & Shapiro, K. L. (1998). Electrophysiological evidence for a postperceptual locus of suppression during the attentional blink. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 24(6), 1656–1674.
3. Sergent, C., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2005). Timing of the brain events underlying access to consciousness during the attentional blink. *Nature Neuroscience*, 8(10), 1391–1400.

### EML-5: Müller et al. (2003) — Steady-State Visual Evoked Potentials (SSVEP) and Attention

**Original Study:** Müller, M. M., Malinowski, P., Gruber, T., & Hillyard, S. A. (2003). Sustained division of the attentional spotlight. *Nature*, 424(6946), 309–312.

**Task Paradigm:** SSVEP Attention. Multiple flickering stimuli at different frequencies are presented simultaneously. Participants attend to one stimulus while ignoring others. EEG power at the flicker frequencies (SSVEP) indexes the allocation of attention.

**Target ERP Component:** **SSVEP** — steady-state visual evoked potential amplitude is enhanced at the frequency of the attended stimulus, providing a continuous, frequency-tagged measure of spatial attention allocation.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Sustained spatial attention
- Feature-based attention
- Attentional spotlight division
- Frequency-tagged neural responses

**Key Finding:** The attentional spotlight can be divided between non-contiguous locations, as shown by enhanced SSVEP amplitudes at frequencies of two attended stimuli separated by an unattended stimulus.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 4 (Visual Search)** and **Task 58 (Multiple Object Tracking)** in terms of distributed attention. This is primarily a **neuroimaging method** (frequency tagging) applied to spatial attention.

**Key References:**
1. Müller, M. M., Malinowski, P., Gruber, T., & Hillyard, S. A. (2003). Sustained division of the attentional spotlight. *Nature*, 424(6946), 309–312.
2. Morgan, S. T., Hansen, J. C., & Hillyard, S. A. (1996). Selective attention to stimulus location modulates the steady-state visual evoked potential. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 93(10), 4770–4774.
3. Andersen, S. K., & Müller, M. M. (2010). Behavioral performance follows the time course of neural facilitation and suppression during cued shifts of feature-selective attention. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 107(31), 13878–13882.

### EML-6: Vogel & Machizawa (2004) — CDA and Visual Working Memory Capacity

**Original Study:** Vogel, E. K., & Machizawa, M. G. (2004). Neural activity predicts individual differences in visual working memory capacity. *Nature*, 428(6984), 748–751.

**Task Paradigm:** Lateralized Change Detection. Participants view a bilateral display with colored items on each side, remember items on the cued side, and detect changes after a brief delay. EEG is recorded from posterior lateral sites during the retention interval.

**Target ERP Component:** **CDA (Contralateral Delay Activity)** — a sustained negativity contralateral to the memorized hemifield during the retention interval. CDA amplitude plateaus at ~3–4 items and predicts individual differences in visual WM capacity (K).

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Visual working memory capacity
- Individual differences in WM
- Neural capacity limits
- Maintenance of visual representations

**Key Finding:** CDA amplitude increases with memory load up to an individual's capacity limit and predicts behavioral K scores, establishing CDA as a neural index of visual WM storage.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 67 (Change Detection Task / Luck-Vogel)**.

**Key References:**
1. Vogel, E. K., & Machizawa, M. G. (2004). Neural activity predicts individual differences in visual working memory capacity. *Nature*, 428(6984), 748–751.
2. Luria, R., Balaban, H., Awh, E., & Vogel, E. K. (2016). The contralateral delay activity as a neural measure of visual working memory. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 62, 100–108.
3. Luck, S. J., & Vogel, E. K. (2013). Visual working memory capacity: From psychophysics and neurobiology to individual differences. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 17(8), 391–400.

### EML-7: Donkers & van Boxtel (2004) — N2 in Go/No-Go: Inhibition vs. Conflict

**Original Study:** Donkers, F. C. L., & van Boxtel, G. J. M. (2004). The N2 in go/no-go tasks reflects conflict monitoring not response inhibition. *Brain and Cognition*, 56(2), 165–176.

**Task Paradigm:** Go/No-Go Task with EEG. Participants respond to frequent go stimuli and withhold responses to infrequent no-go stimuli. Frontocentral N2 is measured on no-go versus go trials.

**Target ERP Component:** **N2 (No-Go N2)** — a frontocentral negativity ~200–350 ms, traditionally attributed to response inhibition but here argued to reflect conflict monitoring rather than inhibition per se.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Response inhibition vs. conflict monitoring
- Go/No-Go performance
- Frontocentral inhibitory processes
- ACC conflict detection

**Key Finding:** The frontocentral N2 in go/no-go tasks reflects conflict between competing response tendencies rather than inhibition of the motor response itself, supported by the observation that N2 amplitude varies with conflict level rather than inhibition demands.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 7 (Go/No-Go Task)**.

**Key References:**
1. Donkers, F. C. L., & van Boxtel, G. J. M. (2004). The N2 in go/no-go tasks reflects conflict monitoring not response inhibition. *Brain and Cognition*, 56(2), 165–176.
2. Falkenstein, M., Hoormann, J., & Hohnsbein, J. (1999). ERP components in Go/Nogo tasks and their relation to inhibition. *Acta Psychologica*, 101(2-3), 267–291.
3. Nieuwenhuis, S., Yeung, N., van den Wildenberg, W., & Ridderinkhof, K. R. (2003). Electrophysiological correlates of anterior cingulate function in a go/no-go task: Effects of response conflict and trial type frequency. *Cognitive, Affective, & Behavioral Neuroscience*, 3(1), 17–26.

### EML-8: Vidal, Hasbroucq, Grapperon, & Bonnet (2000) — Lateralized Readiness Potential

**Original Study:** Vidal, F., Hasbroucq, T., Grapperon, J., & Bonnet, M. (2000). Is the 'error negativity' specific to errors? *Biological Psychology*, 51(2-3), 109–128.

**Task Paradigm:** Choice Reaction Time with LRP. Participants make speeded left/right responses to stimuli. EEG recorded over motor cortex (C3/C4) reveals lateralized readiness potentials preceding the response.

**Target ERP Component:** **LRP (Lateralized Readiness Potential)** — and specifically the relationship between the LRP and the error negativity (Ne/ERN). The study examines whether partial error-related signals appear as sub-threshold LRP deflections on correct trials.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Response preparation and motor programming
- Partial errors and conflict
- Error detection mechanisms
- Lateralized motor cortex activation

**Key Finding:** The "error negativity" is not exclusively tied to overt errors; partial errors (sub-threshold incorrect response activations visible in the LRP on correct trials) also generate an Ne-like deflection, suggesting the Ne reflects response conflict detection rather than error detection per se.

**Task Mapping:** This is primarily a **motor response/ERP methodology** study. Relates to concepts in **Task 1 (Stroop)**, **Task 5 (Flanker)**, and **Task 55 (Simon Task)** — any choice RT task producing the LRP.

**Key References:**
1. Vidal, F., Hasbroucq, T., Grapperon, J., & Bonnet, M. (2000). Is the 'error negativity' specific to errors? *Biological Psychology*, 51(2-3), 109–128.
2. Coles, M. G. H. (1989). Modern mind-brain reading: Psychophysiology, physiology, and cognition. *Psychophysiology*, 26(3), 251–269.
3. De Jong, R., Wierda, M., Mulder, G., & Mulder, L. J. M. (1988). Use of partial stimulus information in response processing. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 14(4), 682–692.

### EML-9: Yeung, Botvinick, & Cohen (2004) — ERN and Conflict Monitoring

**Original Study:** Yeung, N., Botvinick, M. M., & Cohen, J. D. (2004). The neural basis of error detection: Conflict monitoring and the error-related negativity. *Psychological Review*, 111(4), 931–959.

**Task Paradigm:** Flanker Task with EEG. Participants perform an Eriksen Flanker task. EEG is recorded response-locked to examine the ERN on error trials and the correct-response negativity (CRN) on correct trials.

**Target ERP Component:** **ERN (Error-Related Negativity)** — a sharp negative deflection peaking ~50–100 ms after an error response at frontocentral sites (FCz), generated in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC).

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Error detection and monitoring
- Conflict monitoring theory of ACC
- Post-error adjustments
- Performance monitoring

**Key Finding:** The ERN can be explained by a conflict monitoring model: the ACC detects simultaneous activation of the correct and incorrect response representations (conflict), rather than detecting the error itself.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 2 (Eriksen Flanker Task)** and relevant to all conflict/error paradigms.

**Key References:**
1. Yeung, N., Botvinick, M. M., & Cohen, J. D. (2004). The neural basis of error detection: Conflict monitoring and the error-related negativity. *Psychological Review*, 111(4), 931–959.
2. Gehring, W. J., Goss, B., Coles, M. G., Meyer, D. E., & Donchin, E. (1993). A neural system for error detection and compensation. *Psychological Science*, 4(6), 385–390.
3. Botvinick, M. M., Braver, T. S., Barch, D. M., Carter, C. S., & Cohen, J. D. (2001). Conflict monitoring and cognitive control. *Psychological Review*, 108(3), 624–652.

### EML-10: Hajcak, Moser, Yeung, & Simons (2005) — Enhanced ERN in Anxiety

**Original Study:** Hajcak, G., Moser, J. S., Yeung, N., & Simons, R. F. (2005). On the ERN and the significance of errors. *Psychophysiology*, 42(2), 151–160.

**Task Paradigm:** Flanker Task with individual-difference measures. Participants perform a standard arrow Flanker task while EEG is recorded. ERN amplitude is correlated with anxiety measures.

**Target ERP Component:** **ERN** — enhanced (more negative) in individuals high in anxiety and worry, suggesting that anxious individuals have a hypersensitive error-monitoring system.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Error monitoring and anxiety
- Individual differences in performance monitoring
- Worry and error sensitivity
- Defensive motivation

**Key Finding:** ERN amplitude is enhanced in individuals with high trait anxiety and worry, linking error-monitoring neural mechanisms to anxiety-related psychopathology.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 2 (Eriksen Flanker Task)** — same task with individual-difference focus.

**Key References:**
1. Hajcak, G., Moser, J. S., Yeung, N., & Simons, R. F. (2005). On the ERN and the significance of errors. *Psychophysiology*, 42(2), 151–160.
2. Hajcak, G., McDonald, N., & Simons, R. F. (2003). Anxiety and error-related brain activity. *Biological Psychology*, 64(1-2), 77–90.

### EML-11: Hajcak & Foti (2008) — Errors Are Aversive (Affective ERN)

**Original Study:** Hajcak, G., & Foti, D. (2008). Errors are aversive: Defensive motivation and the error-related negativity. *Psychological Science*, 19(2), 103–108.

**Task Paradigm:** Flanker Task with startle probe. Participants perform an arrow Flanker task. Startle probes (acoustic) are delivered after correct and error responses; startle blink magnitude indexes defensive motivation.

**Target ERP Component:** **ERN** — combined with startle potentiation showing that errors activate defensive motivational circuitry. ERN-producing errors potentiate the startle reflex, demonstrating that errors are affectively aversive.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Error monitoring and affect
- Defensive motivation
- Aversive processing of errors
- Startle reflex modulation

**Key Finding:** Startle eyeblink responses are potentiated following errors compared to correct responses, providing converging evidence that errors activate the brain's defensive motivational system, consistent with the affective significance of the ERN.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 2 (Eriksen Flanker Task)**.

**Key References:**
1. Hajcak, G., & Foti, D. (2008). Errors are aversive: Defensive motivation and the error-related negativity. *Psychological Science*, 19(2), 103–108.
2. Hajcak, G. (2012). What we've learned from mistakes: Insights from error-related brain activity. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 21(2), 101–106.
3. Lang, P. J., Bradley, M. M., & Cuthbert, B. N. (1990). Emotion, attention, and the startle reflex. *Psychological Review*, 97(3), 377–395.

### EML-12: Hajcak, McDonald, & Simons (2003) — ERN and Anxiety

**Original Study:** Hajcak, G., McDonald, N., & Simons, R. F. (2003). Anxiety and error-related brain activity. *Biological Psychology*, 64(1-2), 77–90.

**Task Paradigm:** Flanker Task in high vs. low anxiety groups. Participants classified by trait anxiety perform a Flanker task while EEG is recorded.

**Target ERP Component:** **ERN** — larger in high-anxiety participants, providing one of the first demonstrations of anxiety-related ERN enhancement.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Anxiety and error monitoring
- Trait anxiety as moderator of neural function
- Performance monitoring biases
- Clinical ERP biomarkers

**Key Finding:** High-anxious individuals exhibit enhanced ERN amplitudes relative to low-anxious individuals, suggesting a neural basis for the heightened error sensitivity observed behaviorally in anxiety.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 2 (Eriksen Flanker Task)**.

- Individual differences in ACC function

**Key Finding:** Religious conviction is associated with reduced ERN amplitude during Stroop performance, suggesting that meaning-making frameworks can modulate the neural response to errors/conflict.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 1 (Stroop Color-Word Task)**.

### EML-14: Frank, Woroch, & Curran (2005) — FRN and Reinforcement Learning

**Original Study:** Frank, M. J., Woroch, B. S., & Curran, T. (2005). Error-related negativity predicts reinforcement learning and conflict biases. *Neuron*, 47(4), 495–501.

**Task Paradigm:** Probabilistic Stimulus Selection (Frank Task) with EEG. Participants learn to choose between stimuli with probabilistic reward. EEG is recorded during feedback presentation to measure the feedback-related negativity.

**Target ERP Component:** **FRN (Feedback-Related Negativity)** — a frontocentral negativity ~250 ms after negative feedback, reflecting a reward prediction error signal, likely generated by dopaminergic input to ACC.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Reinforcement learning
- Reward prediction error signaling
- Dopaminergic function
- Feedback processing

**Key Finding:** FRN amplitude predicts individual differences in learning from negative vs. positive feedback, linking the ERP component to basal ganglia–mediated reinforcement learning.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 61 (Probabilistic Stimulus Selection / Frank Task)** and **Task 12 (Probabilistic Reversal Learning)**.

### EML-16: Mathewson, Gratton, Fabiani, Beck, & Ro (2009) — Alpha Phase and Visual Perception

**Original Study:** Mathewson, K. E., Gratton, G., Fabiani, M., Beck, D. M., & Ro, T. (2009). To see or not to see: Prestimulus alpha phase predicts visual awareness. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(9), 2725–2732.

**Task Paradigm:** Visual Detection at Threshold. Participants detect near-threshold visual stimuli (metacontrast-masked targets). EEG alpha phase at the moment of stimulus presentation predicts whether the stimulus will be consciously perceived.

**Target EEG Feature:** **Prestimulus Alpha Phase (~10 Hz)** — stimuli presented at the trough of the alpha cycle are more likely to be consciously detected than those at the peak, demonstrating that perception is gated by ongoing oscillatory brain states.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Alpha oscillation and perceptual gating
- Conscious visual awareness
- Prestimulus brain state effects
- Neural excitability cycles

**Key Finding:** Prestimulus alpha phase (~10 Hz) at posterior sites predicts whether near-threshold visual stimuli are consciously detected, supporting pulsed-inhibition theories of alpha function.

**Task Mapping:** This involves a **visual detection task at threshold** — not directly one of the 75 catalog tasks, but conceptually related to consciousness/perception paradigms. New task category: **Metacontrast Masking / Perceptual Threshold Detection**.

**Key References:**
1. Mathewson, K. E., Gratton, G., Fabiani, M., Beck, D. M., & Ro, T. (2009). To see or not to see: Prestimulus alpha phase predicts visual awareness. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(9), 2725–2732.
2. Busch, N. A., Dubois, J., & VanRullen, R. (2009). The phase of ongoing EEG oscillations predicts visual perception. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(24), 7869–7876.
3. VanRullen, R., & Koch, C. (2003). Is perception discrete or continuous? *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(5), 207–213.

### EML-17: Busch, Dubois, & VanRullen (2009) — Prestimulus EEG Phase and Perception

**Original Study:** Busch, N. A., Dubois, J., & VanRullen, R. (2009). The phase of ongoing EEG oscillations predicts visual perception. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(24), 7869–7876.

**Task Paradigm:** Phosphene Detection / Near-Threshold Visual Detection. Participants detect brief, near-threshold flashes. Prestimulus EEG phase and power are analyzed to predict detection success.

**Target EEG Feature:** **Prestimulus Alpha/Theta Phase** — the phase of ongoing ~7–8 Hz and ~10 Hz oscillations at the moment of stimulus delivery predicts whether a near-threshold stimulus will be detected.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Oscillatory gating of perception
- Prestimulus brain state
- Perceptual cycles and rhythmic sampling
- Consciousness threshold mechanisms

**Key Finding:** Visual detection is not random but depends on the phase of ongoing brain oscillations at the time of stimulus delivery, supporting pulsed theories of perception.

**Task Mapping:** Same domain as EML-16. Relates to **threshold detection / perceptual awareness** paradigms.

**Key References:**

### EML-18: Del Cul, Baillet, & Dehaene (2007) — Conscious Access Threshold and P3

**Original Study:** Del Cul, A., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2007). Brain dynamics underlying the nonlinear threshold for access to consciousness. *PLoS Biology*, 5(10), e260.

**Task Paradigm:** Backward Masking with Variable SOA. Participants identify a masked digit presented at varying stimulus-onset asynchronies (SOAs). Visibility transitions nonlinearly from invisible to fully visible around a critical SOA threshold.

**Target ERP Component:** **P3b** — the late positive complex shows a nonlinear all-or-none transition at the consciousness threshold. Early sensory components (P1, N1) scale linearly with SOA, but the P3 emerges abruptly only when consciousness is achieved.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Consciousness threshold
- Global workspace theory (neural correlates)
- Nonlinear ignition in cortical networks
- Backward masking and visibility

**Key Finding:** Access to consciousness is marked by a nonlinear "ignition" of long-range cortical networks indexed by the P3, supporting global workspace theory. Sub-threshold stimuli produce graded sensory responses but fail to trigger the P3.

**Task Mapping:** Related to perceptual threshold/masking paradigms. New task category: **Backward Masking / Consciousness Threshold Detection**.

**Key References:**
1. Del Cul, A., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2007). Brain dynamics underlying the nonlinear threshold for access to consciousness. *PLoS Biology*, 5(10), e260.
2. Dehaene, S., & Changeux, J. P. (2011). Experimental and theoretical approaches to conscious processing. *Neuron*, 70(2), 200–227.
3. Sergent, C., & Dehaene, S. (2004). Is consciousness a gradual phenomenon? Evidence for an all-or-none bifurcation during the attentional blink. *Psychological Science*, 15(11), 720–728.

### EML-19: Sergent, Baillet, & Dehaene (2005) — Temporal Attention and Conscious Perception

**Original Study:** Sergent, C., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2005). Timing of the brain events underlying access to consciousness during the attentional blink. *Nature Neuroscience*, 8(10), 1391–1400.

**Task Paradigm:** Attentional Blink with MEG/EEG. Two targets embedded in an RSVP stream; the second target (T2) is sometimes missed during the attentional blink. Brain responses to seen vs. missed T2 items are compared.

**Target ERP Component:** **P3b / Late Positive Complex** — an all-or-none late positivity (~300–500 ms) that distinguishes consciously perceived ("seen") from unperceived ("blinked") T2 items, while earlier components (N1, N2) are similar.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Conscious access during attentional blink
- All-or-none vs. graded consciousness
- Global workspace ignition
- Temporal attention bottleneck

**Key Finding:** Seen and unseen T2 items diverge sharply at ~270 ms: seen items trigger a late P3-like "ignition," while blinked items do not — consistent with an all-or-none transition to conscious access.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 5 (Attentional Blink Task)**.

**Key References:**
1. Sergent, C., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2005). Timing of the brain events underlying access to consciousness during the attentional blink. *Nature Neuroscience*, 8(10), 1391–1400.
2. Dehaene, S., Sergent, C., & Changeux, J. P. (2003). A neuronal network model linking subjective reports and objective physiological data during conscious perception. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 100(14), 8520–8525.
3. Marti, S., & Dehaene, S. (2017). Discrete and continuous mechanisms of temporal selection in rapid visual streams. *Nature Communications*, 8, 1955.

### EML-20: Onton, Delorme, & Makeig (2005) — Frontal Midline Theta (ICA)

**Original Study:** Onton, J., Delorme, A., & Makeig, S. (2005). Frontal midline EEG dynamics during working memory. *NeuroImage*, 27(2), 341–356.

**Task Paradigm:** Sternberg Working Memory Task with ICA Analysis. Participants perform a Sternberg memory scanning task at variable set sizes. Independent Component Analysis (ICA) is applied to continuous EEG to isolate frontal midline theta activity during memory maintenance.

**Target EEG Feature:** **Frontal Midline Theta (FMθ, ~5–7 Hz)** — theta power over frontal midline sites increases with working memory load and mental effort, reflecting engagement of medial prefrontal / ACC systems.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Working memory maintenance
- Cognitive effort and mental workload
- Frontal theta oscillations
- ICA methodology in EEG

**Key Finding:** ICA isolates a frontal midline component whose theta power parametrically increases with Sternberg set size during the retention interval, providing a clean neural index of working memory engagement.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 14 (Sternberg Item Recognition Task)** and **Task 13 (N-Back Task)**.

**Key References:**

### EML-21: Ganis & Kosslyn (2007) — Mental Rotation ERP

**Original Study:** Ganis, G., & Kosslyn, S. M. (2007). Multiple mechanisms of top-down processing in vision. In S. Bentivoglio et al. (Eds.), *Bentivoglio Lecture Series*. [Note: The specific EEGManyLabs study may reference Ganis, Keveraga, & Kosslyn (2008) on imagery and perception, or Ganis & Kosslyn (2007) on top-down processing.]

**Task Paradigm:** Mental Rotation Task with EEG. Participants view pairs of 3D objects at various angular disparities and judge whether they are the same or mirror images. ERP waveforms are examined as a function of rotation angle.

**Target ERP Component:** **Rotation-Related Negativity (RRN)** — a posterior negativity whose latency and amplitude scale with rotation angle (~400–800 ms), reflecting the duration of the mental rotation process. Also: a late positive component modulated by rotation.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Mental imagery and spatial transformation
- Visuospatial processing
- Analog rotation process
- Top-down imagery mechanisms

**Key Finding:** ERP amplitude and latency show a graded relationship with rotation angle, providing neural evidence that mental rotation operates as an analog spatial transformation process.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 45 (Mental Rotation Task)**.

**Key References:**

### EML-22: Hajcak, Moser, Holroyd, & Simons (2006) — ERN in OCD Spectrum

**Original Study:** Hajcak, G., Moser, J. S., Holroyd, C. B., & Simons, R. F. (2006). It's worse than you thought: The feedback negativity and violations of reward prediction in gambling tasks. *Psychophysiology*, 43(5), 533–538. [Note: Other Hajcak 2006 papers examine ERN and OCD traits.]

**Task Paradigm:** Gambling/Feedback Task with EEG. Participants make choices in a simple gambling task and receive win/loss feedback. EEG is recorded time-locked to feedback.

**Target ERP Component:** **FRN / ERN** — the feedback negativity is enhanced for unexpected losses vs. unexpected wins, reflecting violation of reward predictions.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Reward prediction error
- Outcome monitoring
- Gambling and feedback processing
- Compulsive checking and error sensitivity

**Key Finding:** The FRN reflects reward prediction violations, not just loss processing. Unexpected outcomes of both valences modulate the FRN, with losses producing greater negativity.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 7 (Iowa Gambling Task)** and **Task 61 (PSS / Frank Task)** in terms of feedback processing.

**Key References:**

### EML-23: Eimer & Holmes (2007) — N170 and Face Processing

**Original Study:** Eimer, M., & Holmes, A. (2007). Event-related brain potential correlates of emotional face processing. *Neuropsychologia*, 45(1), 15–31.

**Task Paradigm:** Face/Emotional Face Processing. Participants view faces with different emotional expressions (happy, fearful, neutral) and non-face control objects. EEG is recorded over posterior temporal sites.

**Target ERP Component:** **N170** — a face-sensitive negativity peaking ~170 ms over posterior temporal sites (P7/P8), reflecting structural encoding of faces. Also examined: emotion effects on the N170 and later EPN components.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Face structural encoding
- Emotional expression processing
- Domain-specific face perception
- Early vs. late emotion effects on face processing

**Key Finding:** The N170 is robustly enhanced for faces vs. non-face objects, and emotional expressions (particularly fear) modulate the N170 amplitude, suggesting early interaction between structural and emotional face processing.

**Task Mapping:** Relates to **Task 49 (Face Processing / FFA Localizer)** and **Task 40 (Facial Emotion Recognition)**.

### EML-24: Amodio, Harmon-Jones, Devine, Curtin, Hartley, & Covert (2004) — ERN and Implicit Racial Bias

**Original Study:** Amodio, D. M., Harmon-Jones, E., Devine, P. G., Curtin, J. J., Hartley, S. L., & Covert, A. E. (2004). Neural signals for the detection of unintentional race bias. *Psychological Science*, 15(2), 88–93.

**Task Paradigm:** Weapons Identification Task (Shooter Bias variant) with EEG. Participants categorize ambiguous objects as weapons or tools after viewing Black or White face primes. ERN is measured on trials where race-biased errors occur.

**Target ERP Component:** **ERN** — enhanced on trials where participants make race-biased errors (e.g., misclassifying a tool as a weapon after a Black face prime), reflecting conflict between egalitarian goals and automatic racial stereotypes.

**Cognitive Concepts:**
- Implicit racial bias detection
- Error monitoring and social cognition
- Conflict between controlled and automatic processes
- ACC sensitivity to goal violations

**Key Finding:** ERN amplitude on racially biased error trials predicts subsequent behavioral correction, suggesting that the ACC detects conflicts between egalitarian intentions and automatic stereotype-driven responses.

**Task Mapping:** This combines elements of **implicit association / priming** with error monitoring. Not directly one of the 75 tasks; could be considered a variant of **affective priming** or a **shooter bias / weapons identification** paradigm.

**Key References:**
1. Amodio, D. M., Harmon-Jones, E., Devine, P. G., Curtin, J. J., Hartley, S. L., & Covert, A. E. (2004). Neural signals for the detection of unintentional race bias. *Psychological Science*, 15(2), 88–93.

3. Bartholow, B. D., Pearson, M. A., Dickter, C. L., Sher, K. J., Fabiani, M., & Gratton, G. (2005). Strategic control and medial frontal negativity: Beyond errors and response conflict. *Psychophysiology*, 42(1), 33–42.

## New Tasks Identified

Three task paradigms in the EEGManyLabs set do not map cleanly to the existing 75-task catalog:

1. **Task 76: Perceptual Threshold Detection / Metacontrast Masking** (EML-16, EML-17) — Near-threshold visual detection paradigms examining the role of prestimulus brain states (alpha phase) in determining conscious perception.

2. **Task 77: Backward Masking / Consciousness Threshold** (EML-18) — Variable-SOA backward masking to measure the nonlinear threshold for conscious access, indexing global workspace ignition.

3. **Task 78: Weapons Identification Task (Shooter Bias)** (EML-24) — Race-primed object categorization measuring implicit bias through error patterns and ERN modulation.

## Themes in EEGManyLabs

The 24 studies cluster into several thematic groupings:

1. **Attention** (EML-2, EML-3, EML-4, EML-5, EML-19): Spatial attention (N1), attentional selection (N2pc), sustained/divided attention (SSVEP), temporal attention (attentional blink)
2. **Error Monitoring / Performance Monitoring** (EML-8, EML-9, EML-10, EML-11, EML-12, EML-13, EML-22, EML-24): ERN, conflict monitoring, anxiety × error sensitivity, feedback processing
3. **Consciousness / Perceptual Awareness** (EML-16, EML-17, EML-18, EML-19): Alpha phase effects, backward masking thresholds, all-or-none conscious access
4. **Working Memory** (EML-6, EML-20): CDA and visual WM capacity, frontal theta and WM load
5. **Language** (EML-1): N400 and semantic processing
6. **Emotion** (EML-15, EML-23): Automatic attention to emotional stimuli (EPN), face-emotion perception (N170)
7. **Reinforcement Learning** (EML-14): FRN and reward prediction errors
8. **Motor Preparation** (EML-7, EML-8): Go/No-Go inhibition (N2), lateralized readiness potential
9. **Social Cognition** (EML-24): Implicit bias and error monitoring
10. **Spatial Cognition** (EML-21): Mental rotation ERP
