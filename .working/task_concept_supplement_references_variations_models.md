#### Task 1: Stroop Color-Word Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Egetemeyer, J., Rehme, A. K., Liebhaber, N., Eickhoff, S. B., & Grefkes, C. (2024). Not all Stroop-type tasks are alike: Assessing the impact of stimulus material, task design, and cognitive demand via meta-analyses across neuroimaging studies. *Neuropsychology Review*, 34, 687–714.
2. Servant, M., Montagnini, A., & Burle, B. (2014). Conflict tasks and the diffusion framework: Insight in model constraints based on psychological laws. *Cognitive Psychology*, 72, 162–195.
3. Algom, D., & Chajut, E. (2019). Reclaiming the Stroop effect back from control to input-driven attention and perception. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 10, 1683.
4. Neumann, J., Lohmann, G., Derrfuss, J., & von Cramon, D. Y. (2005). Meta-analysis of functional imaging data using replicator dynamics. *Human Brain Mapping*, 25(1), 165–173. [Stroop neuroimaging meta-analysis]

**Task Variations:**

- **Classic Color-Word Stroop**: Name ink color of incongruent color words; the canonical interference paradigm.
- **Manual (Button-Press) Stroop**: Responses via key press rather than vocal naming; preferred in neuroimaging to reduce motion artifacts.
- **Vocal Response Stroop**: Original format with spoken color-naming responses; produces larger interference due to response-channel overlap with word reading.
- **Emotional Stroop**: Threat-related, disorder-specific, or affectively valenced words replace color words; measures attentional bias to emotional content.
- **Counting Stroop**: Count the number of items displayed while ignoring the number word printed; used extensively in fMRI.
- **Spatial Stroop**: Conflict between stimulus location and directional word content (e.g., "LEFT" on right side).
- **Numerical Stroop**: Conflict between numerical magnitude and physical size of digits.
- **Reverse Stroop**: Read the word while ignoring ink color; reverses the typical control demand.
- **Single-Trial (Event-Related) vs. Blocked Design**: Randomly intermixed trial types vs. condition-blocked presentation; affects proportion-congruent effects and list-wide vs. item-specific control.
- **Proportion-Congruent Manipulation**: Varying the ratio of congruent-to-incongruent trials to study context-driven control adjustments.
- **Face-Word Stroop**: Emotional face images paired with congruent/incongruent emotion labels.
- **Stroop with Conflict Adaptation (Gratton Effect)**: Analyzes sequential trial effects—how preceding trial congruency modulates current-trial interference.
- **Color-Shape Stroop**: Shapes printed in colors that conflict with shape-color associations (e.g., a blue banana).
- **Priming Stroop**: Preceded by neutral, congruent, or incongruent primes to study anticipatory control.
- **Negative Priming Stroop**: Measures inhibitory aftereffects when a previously ignored color word becomes the target on the next trial.

#### Task 2: Eriksen Flanker Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. White, C. N., Servant, M., & Logan, G. D. (2018). Testing the validity of conflict drift-diffusion models for use in estimating cognitive processes: A parameter-recovery study. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 25(1), 323–330.
2. Ulrich, R., Schröter, H., Leuthold, H., & Birngruber, D. (2015). Automatic and controlled stimulus processing in conflict tasks: Superimposed diffusion processes and delta functions. *Cognitive Psychology*, 78, 148–174.
3. Servant, M., & Logan, G. D. (2019). Dynamics of attentional focusing in the Eriksen flanker task. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 81, 2710–2721.
4. Donner, T. H., Siegel, M., Fries, P., & Engel, A. K. (2009). Buildup of choice-predictive activity in human motor cortex during perceptual decision making. *Current Biology*, 19(18), 1581–1585.

**Task Variations:**

- **Arrow Flanker (Standard)**: Five horizontally arranged arrows; identify central arrow direction while ignoring flanking arrows.
- **Letter Flanker**: Letters (e.g., 'H' among 'S' letters) rather than arrows; the original Eriksen format.
- **Color Flanker**: Respond to central stimulus color while ignoring flanking colors.
- **Emotional Flanker**: Emotional faces or images as flankers creating affective interference.
- **Numerical Flanker**: Digit stimuli where flanker magnitude creates conflict with target digit processing.
- **Variable Flanker Distance**: Manipulating spatial separation between target and flankers; closer flankers produce more interference.
- **Variable Set Sizes**: Three, five, or seven stimuli to examine attention scaling.
- **Congruent, Incongruent, and Neutral Conditions**: Standard three-condition design; neutral flankers provide baseline.
- **Proportion-Congruent Flanker**: Varying ratio of congruent to incongruent trials; modulates strategic control engagement.
- **Gratton Effect Analysis**: Sequential trial analysis examining conflict adaptation (post-conflict adjustments).
- **Shrinking Spotlight Model Versions**: Parametric manipulation of flanker eccentricity to test attentional focusing dynamics.
- **Flanker with Speed-Accuracy Tradeoff Manipulation**: Instructions or deadlines manipulating response caution to separate drift rate from threshold effects.
- **Combined Flanker + Go/No-Go**: Flanker conflict combined with response inhibition demands.
- **Reward-Modulated Flanker**: Differential reward/punishment for performance to study motivational effects on conflict processing.

#### Task 3: Posner Spatial Cueing Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Chica, A. B., Martín-Arévalo, E., Botta, F., & Lupiáñez, J. (2014). The Spatial Orienting paradigm: How to design and interpret spatial attention experiments. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 40, 35–51.
2. Theeuwes, J. (2019). Goal-driven, stimulus-driven, and history-driven selection. *Current Opinion in Psychology*, 29, 97–101.
3. Petersen, S. E., & Posner, M. I. (2012). The attention system of the human brain: 20 years after. *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 35, 73–89.
4. Dugué, L., Merriam, E. P., Heeger, D. J., & Carrasco, M. (2020). Differential impact of endogenous and exogenous attention on activity in human visual cortex. *Scientific Reports*, 10, 21274.

**Task Variations:**

- **Exogenous (Peripheral) Cueing**: Abrupt-onset cue at potential target location; captures attention reflexively.
- **Endogenous (Central) Cueing**: Central symbolic cue (arrow, letter) indicating likely target location; requires volitional orienting.
- **Valid, Invalid, and Neutral Cue Conditions**: Standard design decomposing attention effects into costs (invalid minus neutral) and benefits (neutral minus valid).
- **Inhibition of Return (IOR) Paradigm**: Long cue-target SOAs (>300 ms) with peripheral cues revealing inhibitory tagging of previously attended locations.
- **Gap vs. Overlap Conditions**: Fixation point disappears (gap) or remains (overlap) at cue onset; modulates saccade latency and disengagement.
- **Double-Cue Paradigm**: Two successive cues to examine dynamic reorienting.
- **Predictive vs. Non-Predictive Cues**: Cues with varying validity ratios (50%, 75%, 100%) to separate voluntary from reflexive orienting.
- **Reward-Associated Cueing**: Cues associated with differential reward magnitude; examines motivational capture.
- **Feature-Based Cueing**: Cueing attention to a feature dimension (color, orientation) rather than a spatial location.
- **Object-Based Cueing**: Cueing within vs. between objects to study object-based attention.
- **Cross-Modal Cueing**: Auditory or tactile cues directing visual spatial attention.
- **Detection vs. Discrimination Targets**: Simple detection tasks vs. discrimination (identity, orientation) at cued locations.

#### Task 4: Visual Search Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Wolfe, J. M. (2021). Guided Search 6.0: An updated model of visual search. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 28, 1060–1092.
2. Eckstein, M. P. (2011). Visual search: A retrospective. *Journal of Vision*, 11(5), 14.
3. Luck, S. J., & Ford, M. A. (1998). On the role of selective attention in visual perception. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 95(3), 825–830. [Updated by: Liesefeld, H. R., & Müller, H. J. (2019). Distractor handling via dimension weighting. *Current Opinion in Psychology*, 29, 160–167.]
4. Wolfe, J. M., & Horowitz, T. S. (2017). Five factors that guide attention in visual search. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 1, 0058.

**Task Variations:**

- **Feature Search (Pop-Out)**: Target defined by single unique feature; flat set-size functions; parallel processing.
- **Conjunction Search**: Target defined by feature combination; linear set-size functions; serial/guided search.
- **Spatial Configuration Search**: Target defined by spatial arrangement of elements (e.g., rotated T among L's).
- **Efficient vs. Inefficient Search**: Categorized by search slope (ms/item); efficient <10 ms/item, inefficient >25 ms/item.
- **Absent Trials and Target-Present/Absent Ratio**: Manipulating target prevalence; low prevalence produces miss errors (prevalence effect).
- **Multiple-Target Search (Foraging)**: Finding multiple targets in a display; reveals satisfaction-of-search effects.
- **Real-World/Naturalistic Search**: Searching photographs or 3D environments for objects in cluttered natural scenes.
- **Guided Search Variants**: Manipulating top-down guidance via instruction or preview; tests guided search model predictions.
- **Additional-Singleton Paradigm**: Salient but irrelevant distractor captures attention; measures bottom-up capture vs. top-down control.
- **Preview Search**: Half of items presented early (preview); search operates over new items only (visual marking).
- **Repeated Search (Contextual Cueing)**: Repeated spatial configurations facilitate search; implicit spatial learning.
- **RSVP Search (Temporal Search)**: Searching through rapid serial presentation for targets; temporal analog.
- **Eye-Tracking Variants**: Recording fixation patterns, scanpaths, and dwell times during search.
- **Adaptive Choice Visual Search**: Participants choose which display region to search; models foraging decisions.
- **Hybrid Search (Visual + Memory)**: Searching displays for any of multiple targets held in memory; combines visual search and memory search.

#### Task 5: Attentional Blink Task (RSVP)

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Dux, P. E., & Marois, R. (2009). The attentional blink: A review of data and theory. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 71(8), 1683–1700.
2. Willems, C., & Martens, S. (2022). The Attentional Blink at 30: Current and future directions. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 84, 1643–1652.
3. Asplund, C. L., Fougnie, D., Zughni, S., Martin, J. W., & Marois, R. (2014). The attentional blink reveals the probabilistic nature of discrete conscious perception. *Psychological Science*, 25(3), 824–831.
4. Shapiro, K. L., Hanslmayr, S., Enns, J. T., & Lleras, A. (2017). Alpha, beta: The rhythm of the attentional blink. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 24(6), 1862–1869.

**Task Variations:**

- **Standard Dual-Target RSVP**: Two targets embedded in rapid distractor stream (~10 items/sec); report both T1 and T2.
- **Lag Manipulation (Lag 1–8)**: Varying T1–T2 interval; lag-1 sparing, peak blink at lag 2–3.
- **Detection vs. Identification**: T2 requiring simple presence detection vs. full identity report.
- **Three-Target Variant**: Third target added to probe extended temporal limits.
- **Cross-Modal Attentional Blink**: Targets in different modalities (auditory T1, visual T2); tests supramodal bottleneck.
- **Emotional Attentional Blink**: Emotional stimuli (faces, words) as T1 or T2; emotion modulates blink magnitude.
- **Whole-Report RSVP**: Report all items from the stream; reveals temporal dynamics of awareness.
- **Spatial Two-Stream RSVP**: Targets in different spatial streams; separates spatial and temporal attention limits.
- **Blink with Individual Differences**: Correlating blink magnitude with working memory capacity, meditation experience, or personality traits.
- **Attentional Blink Training**: Repeated practice or meditation interventions to reduce blink magnitude.
- **Backward Masking Variant**: Manipulating post-T2 mask presence/absence to separate masking from attentional limitations.

#### Task 6: Continuous Performance Task (CPT)

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Huang-Pollock, C. L., Karalunas, S. L., Tam, H., & Moore, A. N. (2012). Evaluating vigilance deficits in ADHD: A meta-analysis of CPT performance. *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 121(2), 360–371.
2. Esterman, M., Noonan, S. K., Rosenberg, M., & DeGutis, J. (2013). In the zone or zoning out? Tracking behavioral and neural fluctuations during sustained attention. *Cerebral Cortex*, 23(11), 2712–2723.
3. Fortenbaugh, F. C., DeGutis, J., & Esterman, M. (2017). Recent theoretical, neural, and clinical advances in sustained attention research. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 1396(1), 70–91.
4. Weigard, A., & Huang-Pollock, C. (2017). The role of speed in ADHD-related working memory deficits: A time-based resource-sharing and diffusion model account. *Clinical Psychological Science*, 5(2), 195–211.

**Task Variations:**

- **X-CPT (Simple)**: Respond to target letter "X" among non-target letters; measures basic sustained attention.
- **AX-CPT (Context Processing)**: Respond to "X" only when preceded by "A"; probes context maintenance and proactive/reactive control.
- **Identical Pairs CPT (IP-CPT)**: Respond when two consecutive stimuli are identical; taps working memory updating.
- **Gradual-Onset CPT (gradCPT)**: Scene images fade in/out continuously; respond to one category, withhold to another; naturalistic sustained attention.
- **Degraded Stimulus CPT**: Visual noise/blur added to stimuli; increases perceptual difficulty and sustained attention demand.
- **CPT with Varying ISI**: Manipulating inter-stimulus intervals to study temporal expectation effects on vigilance.
- **Auditory CPT**: Auditory stimulus stream; respond to target phoneme or tone.
- **Conners' CPT-3**: Standardized commercial version with norms for ADHD assessment.
- **TOVA (Test of Variables of Attention)**: Commercial CPT variant with specific timing parameters and normative data.
- **Vigilance Decrement Studies**: Extended duration (20–60 min) to track performance decline over time.
- **Parametric Difficulty CPT**: Varying target frequency, ISI, and stimulus degradation systematically.
- **CPT with Emotional Distractors**: Task-irrelevant emotional stimuli during sustained attention.

#### Task 7: Go/No-Go Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Wessel, J. R. (2018). Prepotent motor activity and inhibitory control demands in different variants of the go/no-go paradigm. *Psychophysiology*, 55(3), e12871.
2. Swick, D., Ashley, V., & Turken, U. (2011). Are the neural correlates of stopping and not going identical? Quantitative meta-analysis of two response inhibition tasks. *NeuroImage*, 56(3), 1655–1665.
3. Criaud, M., & Boulinguez, P. (2013). Have we been asking the right questions when assessing response inhibition in go/no-go tasks with fMRI? *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 37(1), 11–23.
4. Littman, R., & Takács, Á. (2017). Do all inhibitions act alike? A study of Go/No-Go and stop-signal paradigms using drift-diffusion modeling. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 8, 321.

**Task Variations:**

- **Standard Go/No-Go**: Frequent go stimuli (75–80%), infrequent no-go stimuli (20–25%).
- **Emotional Go/No-Go**: Emotional faces or words as go/no-go stimuli; probes emotion-cognition interaction.
- **Reward/Punishment Go/No-Go**: Pavlovian-instrumental transfer paradigm—reward biases go, punishment biases no-go.
- **Probabilistic Go/No-Go**: Varying go/no-go ratios (50/50 to 90/10) to manipulate prepotency.
- **Cued Go/No-Go**: Pre-trial cues signal likely go or no-go, allowing proactive control.
- **Flanked Go/No-Go**: Go/no-go stimuli flanked by distractors; combines inhibition and selective attention.
- **Reversal Go/No-Go**: Mid-task contingency reversal; previously-go stimuli become no-go.
- **Go/No-Go with d' Analysis**: Signal detection analysis separating sensitivity from response bias.
- **Saccadic Go/No-Go**: Eye movements as responses; oculomotor inhibition.
- **Sustained (Slow Event-Related) Go/No-Go**: Longer ISIs for fMRI event-related designs.
- **Multi-Stimulus Go/No-Go**: Multiple go stimuli and multiple no-go stimuli; more complex stimulus-response mapping.

#### Task 8: Stop-Signal Task

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Verbruggen, F., Aron, A. R., Band, G. P., Beste, C., et al. (2019). A consensus guide to capturing the ability to inhibit actions and impulsive behaviors in the stop-signal task. *eLife*, 8, e46323.
2. Matzke, D., Curley, S., Gong, Q., & Heathcote, A. (2019). Bayesian modeling of stop-signal reaction time distributions. *Psychological Review*, 126(5), 663–722.
3. Skippen, P., Matzke, D., Heathcote, A., Fulham, W. R., Michie, P., & Karayanidis, F. (2019). Reliability of triggering inhibitory process is a better predictor of impulsivity than SSRT. *Acta Psychologica*, 192, 104–117.
4. Bissett, P. G., Hagen, M. P., Jones, H. M., & Poldrack, R. A. (2021). Design issues and solutions for stop-signal data from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) study. *eLife*, 10, e60185.

**Task Variations:**

- **Standard SST with Staircase SSD**: Dynamic SSD adjustment converging on 50% inhibition probability; yields SSRT via race model.
- **Fixed SSD Procedure**: Predetermined SSDs; simpler but less efficient for SSRT estimation.
- **Stop-Change Task**: Stop signal requires switching to alternative response rather than pure inhibition.
- **Selective Stop-Signal**: Stop signal applies to only one of multiple possible responses; probes response-specific inhibition.
- **Proactive vs. Reactive Inhibition Manipulation**: Varying stop-signal probability across blocks to separate anticipatory from stimulus-triggered control.
- **Bayesian Parametric Estimation of SSRT**: Model-based approaches (ex-Gaussian, BEESTS) for more accurate SSRT estimation.
- **Stop-Signal with fMRI/EEG**: Neuroimaging-adapted versions; critical for studying right inferior frontal and subthalamic contributions.
- **Stop-Signal in Reaching/Saccade Tasks**: Non-button-press responses; extends to oculomotor and reaching domains.
- **Context-Dependent Stop-Signal**: Environmental context predicts stop-signal probability.
- **Conditional Stop-Signal**: Stop signal presented but inhibition required only under certain conditions (e.g., specific signal color).

#### Task 9: Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST)

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Lange, F., Seer, C., & Kopp, B. (2017). Cognitive flexibility in neurological disorders: Cognitive components and event-related potentials. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 83, 496–507.
2. Bishara, A. J., Kruschke, J. K., Stout, J. C., Bechara, A., McCabe, D. P., & Busemeyer, J. R. (2010). Sequential learning models for the Wisconsin Card Sorting Task: Assessing processes in substance dependent individuals. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology*, 54(1), 5–13.

**Task Variations:**

- **Standard WCST (128 Cards)**: Full version with 128 cards, sorting to four reference cards.
- **Short Form WCST (64 Cards)**: Abbreviated version maintaining psychometric properties.
- **Computerized WCST (WCST-CV)**: Digital version with automated scoring.
- **Modified WCST with Explicit Cues**: Varying amounts of information about sorting rules to decompose set-shifting components.
- **Berg Card Sorting Test**: Simplified version used in developmental research.
- **Intra-/Extra-Dimensional Set Shifting (IED)**: CANTAB version explicitly separating intra-dimensional (within-feature) from extra-dimensional (across-feature) shifts.
- **WCST with RL Modeling**: Applying reinforcement learning models to trial-by-trial WCST data to extract learning rates and perseveration parameters.
- **Reversal-Only WCST**: Simplified two-rule version focusing on reversal learning component.
- **Probabilistic WCST**: Feedback is probabilistic rather than deterministic; increases learning difficulty.
- **WCST with Neuroimaging**: Adapted versions for fMRI with modified timing and trial structure.

#### Task 11: Task Switching / Set Shifting Paradigm

**Additional Recent References:**

1. Kiesel, A., Steinhauser, M., Wendt, M., Falkenstein, M., Jost, K., Philipp, A. M., & Koch, I. (2010). Control and interference in task switching—A review. *Psychological Bulletin*, 136(5), 849–874.
2. Vandierendonck, A., Liefooghe, B., & Verbruggen, F. (2010). Task switching: Interplay of reconfiguration and interference control. *Psychological Bulletin*, 136(4), 601–626.
3. Grange, J. A., & Houghton, G. (2014). Models of cognitive control in task switching. In *Task Switching and Cognitive Control* (pp. 160–199). Oxford University Press.
4. Braem, S., & Egner, T. (2018). Getting a grip on cognitive flexibility. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 27(6), 470–476.

**Task Variations:**

- **Cued Task-Switching**: Explicit cue signals task on each trial; separates cue-processing from task-reconfiguration.
- **Alternating Runs (AABB)**: Predictable task sequences without explicit cues; switch costs reflect preparation failures.
- **Voluntary Task Switching**: Participants freely choose which task to perform; reveals internal control biases.
- **Mixing Cost Paradigm**: Comparing single-task blocks to mixed-task blocks; indexes sustained task-set maintenance.
- **Cue-Stimulus Interval (CSI) Manipulation**: Varying preparation time between cue and target; preparation reduces switch costs.
- **Response-Stimulus Interval (RSI) Manipulation**: Varying intertrial interval; affects passive dissipation of prior task set.
- **Task Switching with N>2 Tasks**: Three or more tasks; reveals backward inhibition and n-2 repetition costs.
- **Univalent vs. Bivalent Stimuli**: Stimuli affording response under one task only vs. both tasks.
- **Task Switching with Reward**: Differential incentives for switching vs. repeating; motivational modulation of switch costs.
- **Task Switching under Dual-Task Conditions**: Combining task switching with concurrent secondary task.
- **Predictable vs. Random Task Sequences**: Examining preparation and anticipation effects.
- **Task Switching with Conflict**: Combining task switching with Stroop/flanker-type conflict.

**Task Variations:**

- **Delayed Anti-Saccade**: Maintain fixation during delay after cue before generating antisaccade; increases memory/inhibition demands.
- **Emotional Anti-Saccade**: Emotional faces as cues; measures emotion-driven capture of eye movements.
- **Rewarded Anti-Saccade**: Correct antisaccades differentially rewarded; motivational modulation.
- **Anti-Saccade with Express Saccade Analysis**: Examining short-latency errors as index of automaticity.
- **Memory-Guided Anti-Saccade**: Combine with spatial memory demands.
- **Double-Step Anti-Saccade**: Target location shifts after initial saccade; tests online correction.

#### Task 13: N-Back Task

**Additional Recent References:**

#### Task 51: Digit Span (DS)

**Description:** The Digit Span task measures verbal short-term and working memory capacity. In the Forward condition, participants listen to sequences of digits presented at a rate of one per second and immediately repeat them in the same order; sequence length increases until recall fails. In the Backward condition, participants repeat digits in reverse order, adding a manipulation component. The Sequencing condition (from WAIS-IV) requires reordering digits numerically. Digit Span is one of the oldest and most widely administered neuropsychological measures, embedded in the Wechsler intelligence and memory scales. Forward span primarily indexes the phonological loop, while backward span engages central executive processes.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Verbal short-term memory capacity
- Phonological loop maintenance
- Working memory manipulation (backward)
- Central executive function
- Attention and concentration

#### Task 52: Trail Making Test (TMT)

**Description:** The Trail Making Test is a widely used neuropsychological assessment consisting of two parts. #### Task 53: Rey Auditory Verbal Learning Test (RAVLT)

#### Task 54: Corsi Block-Tapping Task

#### Task 55: Simon Task

**Description:** The Simon Task measures the effect of spatial stimulus-response correspondence on performance. Participants respond to a non-spatial stimulus attribute (e.g., color: press left for red, right for blue) while the stimulus appears on either the left or right side of the screen. The Simon effect reflects automatic activation of a spatially corresponding response that must be overridden when incongruent. The task is a foundational paradigm for studying stimulus-response compatibility and dimensional overlap.

#### Task 56: Navon Task (Global-Local Processing)

#### Task 57: Operation Span Task (OSPAN)

Participants alternate between a processing task (verifying simple math equations, e.g., "Is (2 × 3) + 1 = 8?") and a memory task (remembering letters or words presented after each equation). After a set of 3–7 equation-letter pairs, participants recall the letters in order. The OSPAN score reflects the ability to maintain memory representations while engaging in concurrent processing—a hallmark of working memory capacity (WMC). OSPAN scores are strong predictors of fluid intelligence, reading comprehension, and general executive function.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Working memory capacity
- Dual-task coordination
- Attention control
- Executive function
- Information maintenance under interference

#### Task 58: Multiple Object Tracking (MOT)

**Description:** In the Multiple Object Tracking task, participants view a set of identical objects (typically 8–16 circles), a subset of which are briefly designated as targets (by flashing or color). All objects then move independently and randomly for several seconds, and participants must track the targets among distractors. After movement stops, participants identify which objects were the original targets. Performance is measured as tracking accuracy as a function of target load (typically 1–5 targets). The task provides a direct index of how many individual objects can be simultaneously tracked—a measure of visual attention capacity, estimated at approximately 4 objects.

- Attentional capacity limits

#### Task 59: Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT)

**Description:** The Psychomotor Vigilance Task is a sustained-attention reaction time task widely used in sleep research and fatigue monitoring. Participants monitor a display and respond as quickly as possible to a visual stimulus (typically a millisecond counter) that appears at random inter-stimulus intervals (2–10 seconds) over a 5–10 minute period. The primary measure is the number of lapses (responses >500 ms), reflecting momentary failures of sustained attention. The PVT is considered the gold-standard behavioral measure of vigilance and sleepiness because it is simple, has minimal learning effects, and is highly sensitive to sleep deprivation, circadian misalignment, and time-on-task.

#### Task 60: Dot-Probe Task (Visual Probe)

**Description:** The Dot-Probe Task measures attentional bias toward or away from emotionally salient stimuli. On each trial, a pair of stimuli (e.g., one threatening face and one neutral face) appears briefly (typically 500 ms), followed by a probe (dot or arrow) at the location of one stimulus. Participants respond to the probe (detect or classify it). Attentional bias is inferred from faster responses to probes replacing the emotional stimulus (vigilance toward threat) versus slower responses (avoidance). The task is a cornerstone of research on anxiety-related attentional biases and has been central to Attentional Bias Modification (ABM) treatments.

#### Task 61: Probabilistic Stimulus Selection Task (PSS / Frank Task)

**Description:** The Probabilistic Stimulus Selection Task, developed by Frank and colleagues, assesses the separable contributions of positive and negative reinforcement learning, linked to striatal Go and NoGo pathway function respectively. At test, stimuli are recombined into novel pairs (e.g., A vs. C, B vs. D). Choosing A in novel contexts indexes Go learning (approach from positive feedback), while avoiding B indexes NoGo learning (avoidance from negative feedback). The task was specifically designed to probe basal ganglia dopaminergic mechanisms and is sensitive to dopaminergic medication effects in Parkinson's disease.

#### Task 62: Prisoner's Dilemma

**Description:** The Prisoner's Dilemma is a canonical game theory paradigm for studying cooperation and defection under strategic interdependence. Two players simultaneously choose to cooperate or defect. Mutual cooperation yields moderate rewards for both; mutual defection yields low rewards for both; but if one defects while the other cooperates, the defector receives the highest reward and the cooperator receives nothing (or a penalty). The dominant strategy in single-shot play is to defect, yet human participants frequently cooperate, particularly in iterated versions. The task is central to research on trust, reciprocity, altruism, and the neural substrates of social decision-making.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**

#### Task 63: Monetary Incentive Delay Task (MID)

**Description:** The Monetary Incentive Delay Task is the dominant paradigm for neuroimaging studies of reward anticipation and consumption. The MID has been widely adopted in clinical neuroimaging of addiction, depression, and schizophrenia.

#### Task 64: Radial Arm Maze (Virtual)

#### Task 65: Mirror Tracing Task (MTT)

**Description:** The Mirror Tracing Task measures procedural motor learning and sensorimotor adaptation. Participants trace a shape (typically a star outline) while viewing their hand only through a mirror, which reverses the visual feedback left-right. This creates a conflict between visual and proprioceptive information that must be resolved through practice. Performance improves across trials as participants learn to adapt their motor commands to the reversed visual input. Measures include tracing time, number of errors (deviations from the path), and the rate of improvement across trials. The task is a classic demonstration that procedural/implicit memory is preserved in amnesia (H.M. could learn this task despite profound declarative memory loss).

#### Task 66: Cambridge Face Memory Test (CFMT)

#### Task 67: Change Detection Task (Luck-Vogel)

**Description:** The Change Detection Task, developed by Luck and Vogel (1997), is the standard paradigm for measuring visual working memory capacity. Participants briefly view a memory array of colored squares (typically 100–500 ms), followed by a blank retention interval (~900 ms), then a test array. On half the trials, one item changes color; participants report whether a change occurred. Performance is converted to a capacity estimate (K) using Cowan's formula: K = set size × (hit rate − false alarm rate). The task established that visual WM capacity is approximately 3–4 items in healthy adults and has been instrumental in research linking WM capacity to attention, intelligence, and neural oscillations.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**

- Feature binding in WM
- Change detection sensitivity
- Attentional filtering of irrelevant items
- Individual differences in cognitive ability

**Additional Recent References:**

#### Task 68: Useful Field of View (UFOV)

#### Task 69: Sustained Attention to Response Task (SART)

**Description:** The Sustained Attention to Response Task is a Go/No-Go variant specifically designed to measure sustained attention failures and mind-wandering. #### Task 71: Mnemonic Similarity Task (MST / Mnemonic Discrimination Task)

#### Task 73: Wason Selection Task

**Description:** The Wason Selection Task is the classic test of conditional reasoning. Participants see four cards, each showing information on both sides. - **Timed vs. Untimed RAT**: Strict time limits vs. self-paced to study incubation effects.
- **RAT with Hint/Priming**: Subliminal or suprathreshold presentation of the answer or related words.
- **Warmth Ratings**: Participants rate "closeness to solution" during problem-solving; feeling-of-warmth paradigm.
- **RAT in Different Languages**: Translated/adapted versions (e.g., Japanese, German, Italian RAT).
- **EEG/fMRI RAT**: Neural correlates of insight moments (gamma burst, right temporal activation).
- **RAT with Mood Manipulation**: Positive vs. negative mood induction before solving; mood-creativity link.
- **RAT as Individual Differences Measure**: Correlating RAT scores with personality, intelligence, and creativity tests.

#### Task 75: Social Incentive Delay Task (SID)

**Description:** The Social Incentive Delay Task is a variant of the Monetary Incentive Delay Task (Task 63) that replaces monetary outcomes with social rewards and punishments. Cues signal the potential for positive social feedback (e.g., a smiling face with a thumbs-up), negative social feedback (e.g., a frowning face with a thumbs-down), or neutral outcomes. Participants then respond to a target stimulus, and outcome delivery depends on response speed. The SID was developed to compare the neural substrates of social versus non-social reward anticipation and consumption, testing whether social rewards engage the same striatal dopaminergic circuitry as monetary rewards. The task has been particularly impactful in autism spectrum disorder research, where reduced social reward sensitivity is hypothesized.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Social reward anticipation and consumption
- Social motivation
- Comparison of social vs. monetary reward processing
- Ventral striatal response to social incentives
- Social anhedonia

### 4.10 New Tasks from EEGManyLabs Replication Studies

The following three tasks were identified through the EEGManyLabs replication project (Pavlov et al., 2021) as paradigms that do not map cleanly to the preceding 75 tasks. Each represents a distinct experimental paradigm that has become influential in cognitive neuroscience.

#### Task 76: Perceptual Threshold Detection / Metacontrast Masking

**Description:** Participants attempt to detect or discriminate brief, near-threshold visual stimuli that are rendered difficult to see through low contrast, brief duration, or metacontrast masking (a spatially adjacent, temporally trailing mask that suppresses visibility of the target without overlapping it spatially). The key dependent variable is not only accuracy but the relationship between trial-by-trial detection success and prestimulus brain states — particularly the phase and power of ongoing alpha (~10 Hz) and theta (~7 Hz) oscillations recorded via EEG. This paradigm demonstrates that conscious visual perception is not a passive, deterministic process but is gated by rhythmic fluctuations in cortical excitability, supporting pulsed-inhibition and perceptual-sampling theories.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Perceptual awareness and consciousness threshold
- Alpha oscillation and perceptual gating (pulsed inhibition)
- Prestimulus brain state effects on detection
- Rhythmic perceptual sampling
- Metacontrast masking mechanisms
- Neural excitability cycles and cortical gain modulation

#### Task 77: Backward Masking / Consciousness Threshold Detection

**Description:** Participants identify or detect a briefly presented target stimulus (typically a digit, letter, or shape) followed by a backward pattern mask at variable stimulus-onset asynchronies (SOAs). As SOA increases from very short (< 20 ms) to long (> 100 ms), target visibility transitions from invisible to fully visible. The critical finding is that this transition is nonlinear — subjective visibility and neural signatures (particularly the P3b component and late widespread cortical activation) show an abrupt, all-or-none "ignition" at a threshold SOA rather than a gradual increase. Early sensory components (P1, N1) scale linearly with SOA, but the P3 emerges only when conscious access is achieved. This paradigm provides key evidence for the Global Neuronal Workspace theory of consciousness.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Consciousness threshold and access consciousness
- Global Neuronal Workspace ignition
- Nonlinear transitions in cortical processing
- Backward masking and temporal integration
- Dissociation of sensory processing from conscious access
- Subliminal vs. supraliminal processing

**Key References:**
1. Del Cul, A., Baillet, S., & Dehaene, S. (2007). Brain dynamics underlying the nonlinear threshold for access to consciousness. *PLoS Biology*, 5(10), e260.

3. Sergent, C., & Dehaene, S. (2004). Is consciousness a gradual phenomenon? Evidence for an all-or-none bifurcation during the attentional blink. *Psychological Science*, 15(11), 720–728.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Mashour, G. A., Roelfsema, P., Changeux, J. P., & Dehaene, S. (2020). Conscious processing and the global neuronal workspace hypothesis. *Neuron*, 105(5), 776–798.

**Task Variations:**
- **Variable-SOA Backward Pattern Masking**: Standard version; random-dot or hash-mark mask at 8–100 ms SOA.
- **Metacontrast Masking Version**: Non-overlapping annular mask; different masking mechanism producing U-shaped masking functions.
- **Forced-Choice + Subjective Rating**: Combining objective performance (4AFC digit identification) with subjective visibility scale (PAS or VAS) to dissociate objective and subjective thresholds.
- **Masked Priming Variant**: Masked stimuli serve as primes for subsequent visible targets; measures subliminal processing below the consciousness threshold.

#### Task 78: Weapons Identification Task (Shooter Bias)

**Description:** Participants view face primes (typically Black and White faces, presented briefly) followed by target objects that are either weapons (guns, knives) or non-threatening objects (tools, household items). This paradigm bridges social psychology, implicit cognition, and cognitive neuroscience by providing a neural signature of bias detection.

## Part II: Expanded Cognitive Models and Theoretical Frameworks

This section substantially expands the original document's survey from 11 to approximately 40 cognitive models and frameworks, organized by domain. Each model includes a description of core mechanisms and 2–3 key references.

### Attention Models

---

#### 6.12 Feature Integration Theory (Treisman)

Feature Integration Theory proposes a two-stage model of visual attention. In the preattentive stage, basic visual features (color, orientation, size, motion) are processed automatically in parallel across the visual field without requiring focal attention. In the focused attention stage, attention acts as a "binding spotlight" that integrates individual features together into coherent object percepts via a master map of spatial locations. The theory explains the pop-out effect in feature search (parallel), the serial nature of conjunction search (requiring attention for binding), and illusory conjunctions (erroneous feature binding under attentional load). It is directly operationalized by the visual search task (Task 4) and underpins much of the visual attention literature.

**References:**
- Treisman, A., & Gelade, G. (1980). A feature-integration theory of attention. *Cognitive Psychology*, 12(1), 97–136.
- Treisman, A. (1998). Feature binding, attention and object perception. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 353(1373), 1295–1306.

---

#### 6.13 Guided Search Model (Wolfe)

Guided Search 6.0 is a hybrid serial-parallel model proposing that visual search involves a preattentive parallel stage that computes feature-based activation across the display, generating a spatial priority map. Selective attention is then deployed serially to locations in order of priority map activation. Guidance comes from five sources: bottom-up salience, top-down feature guidance, scene-based guidance, history-driven selection, and reward-driven selection. The model accounts for search efficiency differences across task types, set-size effects, and the prevalence effect in which rare targets are more likely to be missed. It has been progressively updated over 30 years of visual search research.

**References:**
- Wolfe, J. M. (2021). Guided Search 6.0: An updated model of visual search. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 28, 1060–1092.
- Wolfe, J. M., & Horowitz, T. S. (2017). Five factors that guide attention in visual search. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 1, 0058.
- Wolfe, J. M. (1994). Guided Search 2.0: A revised model of visual search. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 1(2), 202–238.

---

#### 6.14 Load Theory of Attention (Lavie)

Load Theory proposes that the efficiency of selective attention depends on the level and type of information processing load. Under high perceptual load (many items or complex stimuli), perception operates at capacity, leaving no resources for processing distractors—thus selection is efficient. Under low perceptual load, spare capacity "spills over" to process irrelevant distractors, causing interference. Conversely, high cognitive load (taxing working memory or executive control) impairs the ability to maintain task priorities, increasing distractor processing. The theory resolves the classic early- vs. late-selection debate by proposing that both can occur depending on load conditions.

**References:**
- Lavie, N. (1995). Perceptual load as a necessary condition for selective attention. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 21(3), 451–468.
- Lavie, N. (2005). Distracted and confused? Selective attention under load. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 9(2), 75–82.
- Lavie, N., Hirst, A., de Fockert, J. W., & Viding, E. (2004). Load theory of selective attention and cognitive control. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 133(3), 339–354.

---

#### 6.15 Multiple Demand Network (Duncan)

The Multiple Demand (MD) network is a set of frontoparietal brain regions (including dorsolateral PFC, anterior insula/frontal operculum, dorsal ACC/pre-SMA, and intraparietal sulcus) that are co-activated across a vast range of cognitively demanding tasks, regardless of specific content. Duncan proposed that these regions instantiate a domain-general "adaptive coding" system: neurons in MD regions flexibly adjust their tuning to represent whatever information is currently task-relevant. The MD network is strongly associated with fluid intelligence (g-factor) and is recruited by executive function tasks including Stroop, flanker, task switching, and working memory paradigms.

**References:**
- Duncan, J. (2010). The multiple-demand (MD) system of the primate brain: Mental programs for intelligent behaviour. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 14(4), 172–179.
- Fedorenko, E., Duncan, J., & Kanwisher, N. (2013). Broad domain generality in focal regions of frontal and parietal cortex. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 110(41), 16616–16621.
- Duncan, J. (2001). An adaptive coding model of neural function in prefrontal cortex. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 2(11), 820–829.

---

#### 6.16 Attention Schema Theory (Graziano)

Attention Schema Theory (AST) proposes that the brain constructs an internal model of its own attention processes—an "attention schema"—analogous to how the body schema models the body. This schema is a simplified, sometimes inaccurate model of the brain's attentional state, and it forms the basis for subjective awareness. When the brain models its own attention to a stimulus, the result is the conscious experience of that stimulus. AST provides a mechanistic account of consciousness emerging from attention mechanisms and has implications for understanding the relationship between attention and awareness across many experimental tasks.

**References:**
- Graziano, M. S. A., & Kastner, S. (2011). Human consciousness and its relationship to social neuroscience: A novel hypothesis. *Cognitive Neuroscience*, 2(2), 98–113.
- Graziano, M. S. A. (2013). *Consciousness and the Social Brain*. Oxford University Press.

### Memory Models

---

#### 6.17 Complementary Learning Systems (CLS)

The CLS framework proposes that effective learning requires two complementary neural systems with fundamentally different learning properties. The hippocampus supports rapid, one-shot learning of specific episodes with sparse, pattern-separated representations that minimize interference between similar memories. The neocortex learns slowly through interleaved experience, gradually extracting statistical regularities and building structured semantic knowledge. Memory consolidation transfers information from hippocampus to neocortex over time, and replay during sleep plays a key role. The theory accounts for phenomena including catastrophic interference in neural networks, consolidation gradients, and the differential roles of hippocampal and cortical systems in memory tasks.

**References:**
- McClelland, J. L., McNaughton, B. L., & O'Reilly, R. C. (1995). Why there are complementary learning systems in the hippocampus and neocortex. *Psychological Review*, 102(3), 419–457.
- Kumaran, D., Hassabis, D., & McClelland, J. L. (2016). What learning systems do intelligent agents need? Complementary learning systems theory updated. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 20(7), 512–534.
- O'Reilly, R. C., Bhatt, M. A., & Frank, M. J. (2012). Making working memory work: A computational model of learning in the prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia. *Neural Computation*, 18(2), 283–328.

---

#### 6.18 Context Maintenance and Retrieval (CMR) Model

The CMR model (and its predecessor TCM, the Temporal Context Model) accounts for free recall by proposing that items are associated with a gradually drifting temporal context representation. During encoding, each item is bound to the current context state and also updates that context. During retrieval, recalling an item reinstates its associated context, which in turn cues neighboring items. The model explains serial position effects, temporal contiguity effects (tendency to recall items studied nearby in time), and semantic clustering. CMR extends TCM by incorporating semantic similarity to explain category clustering in recall and has been fit to extensive behavioral datasets.

**References:**
- Polyn, S. M., Norman, K. A., & Kahana, M. J. (2009). A context maintenance and retrieval model of organizational processes in free recall. *Psychological Review*, 116(1), 129–156.
- Howard, M. W., & Kahana, M. J. (2002). A distributed representation of temporal context. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology*, 46(3), 269–299.
- Kahana, M. J. (2020). Computational models of memory search. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 71, 107–138.

---

#### 6.19 Search of Associative Memory (SAM) / Retrieving Effectively from Memory (REM)

SAM is a classic mathematical model of memory search proposing that retrieval involves sampling items from memory based on their associative strength to a retrieval cue, followed by a recovery (recognition) process. Items are stored as vectors of feature values, and retrieval probability depends on the match between cue features and stored representations. REM extends SAM by incorporating a Bayesian framework: recognition memory decisions are based on a likelihood ratio comparing the odds that a test probe was studied versus new. Both models account for list-strength effects, list-length effects, and word-frequency mirror effects in recognition memory, and have been enormously influential in mathematical modeling of memory.

**References:**
- Raaijmakers, J. G. W., & Shiffrin, R. M. (1981). Search of associative memory. *Psychological Review*, 88(2), 93–134.
- Shiffrin, R. M., & Steyvers, M. (1997). A model for recognition memory: REM—Retrieving effectively from memory. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 4(2), 145–166.

---

#### 6.20 Fuzzy Trace Theory (Brainerd & Reyna)

Fuzzy Trace Theory proposes that experiences are encoded in parallel as two distinct memory traces: verbatim traces (exact, detailed representations of surface form) and gist traces (fuzzy, meaning-based representations of essential content). The two traces are stored independently and retrieved by different processes. A key claim is that decision-making and memory retrieval preferentially rely on gist (fuzzy) representations rather than verbatim (precise) ones, a "fuzzy-processing preference." The theory accounts for developmental reversals in false memory (children sometimes show fewer false memories than adults because they rely more on verbatim traces), the DRM false memory effect, and framing effects in decision-making.

**References:**
- Brainerd, C. J., & Reyna, V. F. (2002). Fuzzy-trace theory and false memory. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 11(5), 164–169.
- Reyna, V. F., & Brainerd, C. J. (1995). Fuzzy-trace theory: An interim synthesis. *Learning and Individual Differences*, 7(1), 1–75.

---

#### 6.21 Systems Consolidation Theory

Systems Consolidation Theory proposes that newly formed hippocampus-dependent memories are gradually reorganized over time (days to years) to become increasingly dependent on neocortical storage. The standard model (Squire) holds that hippocampal involvement decreases as cortical representations are strengthened through replay and reconsolidation processes, eventually rendering memories independent of the hippocampus. The multiple trace theory (Nadel & Moscovitch) counters that hippocampal involvement persists for detailed episodic memories while only semantic/schematic aspects become hippocampus-independent. Sleep replay, particularly during slow-wave sleep, is thought to drive consolidation by reactivating hippocampal memory traces.

**References:**
- Squire, L. R., & Alvarez, P. (1995). Retrograde amnesia and memory consolidation: A neurobiological perspective. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 5(2), 169–177.
- Nadel, L., & Moscovitch, M. (1997). Memory consolidation, retrograde amnesia and the hippocampal complex. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 7(2), 217–227.
- Frankland, P. W., & Bontempi, B. (2005). The organization of recent and remote memories. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 6(2), 119–130.

### Decision and Accumulation Models

---

#### 6.22 Linear Ballistic Accumulator (LBA)

The LBA is a simplified evidence accumulation model for choice tasks that assumes deterministic (ballistic, noise-free) evidence accumulation within each trial. On each trial, accumulators for each response option race toward a common threshold; the first to reach threshold determines the choice and RT. Variability across trials comes from random variation in starting points and drift rates, drawn from specified distributions. The LBA is mathematically simpler than the DDM (closed-form likelihood function), making it computationally tractable for complex tasks with multiple choice options. It has been applied to lexical decision, recognition memory, perceptual discrimination, and multi-alternative choice paradigms.

**References:**
- Brown, S. D., & Heathcote, A. (2008). The simplest complete model of choice response time: Linear ballistic accumulation. *Cognitive Psychology*, 57(3), 153–178.
- Donkin, C., Brown, S., Heathcote, A., & Wagenmakers, E. J. (2011). Diffusion versus linear ballistic accumulation: Different models but the same conclusions about psychological processes? *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 18(1), 61–69.

---

#### 6.23 Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

Signal Detection Theory provides a formal framework for analyzing decisions under uncertainty, separating the observer's perceptual sensitivity (d') from their response criterion (c or β). Applied to cognitive tasks, it distinguishes the ability to discriminate signal from noise (sensitivity) from the willingness to report detection (bias). SDT produces receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curves that characterize the full tradeoff between hits and false alarms. It is fundamental to recognition memory (old/new discrimination), perception (detection thresholds), medical diagnosis, and any task requiring binary classification under uncertainty. The framework has been extended to confidence ratings, multi-alternative decisions, and neural population coding.

**References:**
- Green, D. M., & Swets, J. A. (1966). *Signal Detection Theory and Psychophysics*. Wiley.
- Macmillan, N. A., & Creelman, C. D. (2005). *Detection Theory: A User's Guide* (2nd ed.). Lawrence Erlbaum.
- Wixted, J. T. (2020). The forgotten history of signal detection theory. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 46(2), 201–233.

---

#### 6.24 Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky)

Prospect Theory describes how people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point rather than in terms of absolute wealth. It proposes two key departures from expected utility theory: (1) a value function that is concave for gains and convex for losses (diminishing sensitivity) and steeper for losses than gains (loss aversion), and (2) a probability weighting function that overweights small probabilities and underweights large ones. These features explain the certainty effect, the reflection effect, framing effects, and the endowment effect. Cumulative Prospect Theory (1992) extended the model to handle multi-outcome gambles. The theory is central to understanding decision-making tasks including the IGT, BART, and gambling paradigms.

**References:**
- Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A. (1979). Prospect theory: An analysis of decision under risk. *Econometrica*, 47(2), 263–291.
- Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1992). Advances in prospect theory: Cumulative representation of uncertainty. *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*, 5(4), 297–323.
- Fox, C. R., & Poldrack, R. A. (2014). Prospect theory and the brain. In *Neuroeconomics* (2nd ed., pp. 533–567). Academic Press.

#### 6.25 Race Model of Inhibition (Logan & Cowan)

The Race Model formalizes the stop-signal task as a race between a "go" process (initiated by the go stimulus) and a "stop" process (initiated by the stop signal). Whichever process finishes first determines the behavioral outcome. The model assumes stochastic independence between go and stop processes, allowing estimation of the covert stop-signal reaction time (SSRT) from observable go RTs and inhibition probability. The race model has been the dominant theoretical framework for interpreting stop-signal task data for 40 years and has been extended with parametric models (ex-Gaussian SSRT distributions) and Bayesian estimation approaches.

**References:**
- Logan, G. D., & Cowan, W. B. (1984). On the ability to inhibit thought and action: A theory of an act of control. *Psychological Review*, 91(3), 295–327.
- Verbruggen, F., & Logan, G. D. (2009). Models of response inhibition in the stop-signal and stop-change paradigms. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 33(5), 647–661.
- Band, G. P., van der Molen, M. W., & Logan, G. D. (2003). Horse-race model simulations of the stop-signal procedure. *Acta Psychologica*, 112(2), 105–142.

#### 6.26 Urgency-Gating Model

The Urgency-Gating Model proposes that decision-making involves combining a static sample of evidence quality with a growing urgency signal that increases over time. Unlike standard drift-diffusion models where evidence accumulates over time, urgency-gating suggests that the brain takes a time-limited snapshot of evidence and multiplies it by an urgency signal that pushes the system toward a decision. The model accounts for speed-accuracy tradeoffs, collapsing decision boundaries, and neural data showing urgency signals in premotor and decision-related cortex. It provides an alternative to pure evidence-accumulation accounts of tasks like the random dot motion discrimination.

**References:**
- Cisek, P., Puskas, G. A., & El-Murr, S. (2009). Decisions in changing conditions: The urgency-gating model. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 29(37), 11560–11571.
- Thura, D., Beauregard-Racine, J., Fradet, C. W., & Bhatt, P. (2012). Decision making by urgency gating: Theory and experimental support. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 108(11), 2912–2930.

### Language Models

---

#### 6.27 Dual-Route Cascaded Model (DRC; Coltheart)

The DRC model proposes two routes for reading aloud: a lexical route that retrieves whole-word pronunciations from a mental lexicon (necessary for irregular words like "yacht"), and a sublexical/grapheme-phoneme conversion route that converts letters to sounds via rules (necessary for novel nonwords). The two routes operate in cascade (partial activation feeds forward before processing completes) and interact. The model accounts for the regularity effect (irregular words slower than regular), frequency effect, and nonword reading ability. It simulates acquired and developmental dyslexia patterns as selective damage to one route.

**References:**
- Coltheart, M., Rastle, K., Perry, C., Langdon, R., & Ziegler, J. (2001). DRC: A dual route cascaded model of visual word recognition and reading aloud. *Psychological Review*, 108(1), 204–256.
- Coltheart, M. (2005). Modeling reading: The dual-route approach. In *The Science of Reading: A Handbook* (pp. 6–23). Blackwell.

---

#### 6.28 Interactive Activation Model (McClelland & Rumelhart)

The Interactive Activation (IA) model of visual word recognition proposes three levels of processing: features, letters, and words, connected by excitatory and inhibitory links. Activation cascades both bottom-up (features → letters → words) and top-down (words → letters), creating interactive feedback. The model explains the word superiority effect (letters identified better in words than isolation), word frequency effects, and neighborhood effects. It was one of the first connectionist models to demonstrate how emergent, knowledge-driven processing could arise from simple local computations without explicit rules.

**References:**
- McClelland, J. L., & Rumelhart, D. E. (1981). An interactive activation model of context effects in letter perception: Part 1. An account of basic findings. *Psychological Review*, 88(5), 375–407.
- Grainger, J., & Jacobs, A. M. (1996). Orthographic processing in visual word recognition: A multiple read-out model. *Psychological Review*, 103(3), 518–565.

---

#### 6.29 WEAVER++ (Levelt, Roelofs, Meyer)

WEAVER++ (Word Encoding by Activation and VERification) is a computational model of word production spanning conceptual preparation through articulation. It specifies three strata: conceptual (lemma selection based on semantic activation), word-form encoding (morphological and phonological encoding), and phonetic/articulatory encoding. The model assumes a serial-discrete flow for lemma access but cascaded processing for phonological encoding. It successfully simulates picture naming latencies, semantic and phonological facilitation/interference in picture-word tasks, and speech error patterns. WEAVER++ is the most comprehensive computational model of language production.

**References:**
- Levelt, W. J. M., Roelofs, A., & Meyer, A. S. (1999). A theory of lexical access in speech production. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 22(1), 1–38.
- Roelofs, A. (2003). Goal-referenced selection of verbal action: Modeling attentional control in the Stroop task. *Psychological Review*, 110(1), 88–125.

#### 6.30 Surprisal / Expectation-Based Models

Surprisal theory proposes that processing difficulty in language comprehension is proportional to the surprisal (negative log probability) of each word given prior context: words that are less predictable from context are harder to process. This information-theoretic framework naturally predicts reading time patterns across a wide range of sentence constructions. Levy's expectation-based model formalizes this as a probabilistic parser that maintains a distribution over possible syntactic structures and updates it with each word; garden-path difficulty reflects the probability mass that must be reallocated during reanalysis. Large language models (GPT, etc.) have been used to compute surprisal estimates that correlate with human reading times.

**References:**
- Hale, J. (2001). A probabilistic Earley parser as a psycholinguistic model. In *Proceedings of NAACL* (pp. 1–8).
- Levy, R. (2008). Expectation-based syntactic comprehension. *Cognition*, 106(3), 1126–1177.
- Smith, N. J., & Levy, R. (2013). The effect of word predictability on reading time is logarithmic. *Cognition*, 128(3), 302–319.

### Learning and Category Models

---

#### 6.31 COVIS (Competition between Verbal and Implicit Systems)

COVIS proposes that category learning is mediated by two competing systems: an explicit, verbal/hypothesis-testing system that learns categories defined by simple rules (mediated by PFC and head of caudate), and an implicit, procedural system that learns categories requiring integration of information across multiple dimensions (mediated by the body/tail of caudate and associated striatal circuits). The two systems compete for control of behavior, with the explicit system dominating early in learning and the implicit system gaining influence for categories that cannot be captured by simple rules. COVIS accounts for dissociations between rule-based and information-integration category learning across clinical populations and experimental manipulations.

**References:**
- Ashby, F. G., Alfonso-Reese, L. A., Turken, A. U., & Waldron, E. M. (1998). A neuropsychological theory of multiple systems in category learning. *Psychological Review*, 105(3), 442–481.
- Ashby, F. G., & Maddox, W. T. (2005). Human category learning. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 56, 149–178.

---

#### 6.32 Exemplar Theory / Generalized Context Model (GCM; Nosofsky)

The GCM assumes that categories are represented by stored exemplars (individual memory traces of category members) rather than summary statistics or prototypes. Categorization of a new item depends on its similarity to all stored exemplars of each category, computed as a weighted sum across dimensions. The model includes selective attention parameters that weight dimensions by their diagnostic relevance. The GCM accounts for typicality gradients, the effects of category size, within-category similarity structure, and individual differences in categorization strategy. It has been applied to perceptual categorization, face recognition, and diagnostic reasoning.

**References:**
- Nosofsky, R. M. (1986). Attention, similarity, and the identification-categorization relationship. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 115(1), 39–61.
- Nosofsky, R. M. (2011). The generalized context model: An exemplar model of classification. In *Formal Approaches in Categorization* (pp. 18–39). Cambridge University Press.

### Motor and Action Models

---

#### 6.33 Optimal Feedback Control Theory

Optimal Feedback Control Theory proposes that the nervous system controls movements by optimizing a cost function that trades off accuracy, effort, and time. Rather than planning a fixed trajectory, the controller continuously adjusts motor commands based on sensory feedback about the current state, using state estimation (Kalman filter) and optimal control policies. The minimum intervention principle states that the controller only corrects deviations that interfere with task goals, allowing variability in task-irrelevant dimensions. The framework accounts for reaching trajectories, grip force control, postural adjustments, and the speed-accuracy tradeoff.

**References:**
- Todorov, E., & Jordan, M. I. (2002). Optimal feedback control as a theory of motor coordination. *Nature Neuroscience*, 5(11), 1226–1235.
- Scott, S. H. (2004). Optimal feedback control and the neural basis of volitional motor control. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 5(7), 532–546.

---

#### 6.34 Internal Forward/Inverse Models

The internal models framework proposes two complementary computational elements for motor control. Forward models predict the sensory consequences of motor commands before feedback arrives, enabling rapid movement correction and the sense of agency. Inverse models compute the motor commands needed to achieve a desired sensory state. Forward models in the cerebellum are thought to cancel predicted sensory reafference (why you can't tickle yourself), enable rapid online correction during reaching, and support motor learning by generating prediction errors. Inverse models translate goals into appropriate motor programs.

**References:**
- Wolpert, D. M., & Ghahramani, Z. (2000). Computational principles of movement neuroscience. *Nature Neuroscience*, 3(Suppl), 1212–1217.
- Wolpert, D. M., Miall, R. C., & Kawato, M. (1998). Internal models in the cerebellum. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 2(9), 338–347.
- Kawato, M. (1999). Internal models for motor control and trajectory planning. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 9(6), 718–727.

### Integrative and Large-Scale Models

---

#### 6.35 Connectionism / Parallel Distributed Processing (PDP)

PDP/connectionist models propose that cognitive processes emerge from the interaction of many simple, neuron-like processing units connected by weighted links. Knowledge is stored in connection weights rather than symbolic rules, and learning occurs through gradient-descent adjustment of these weights (e.g., backpropagation). Key properties include graceful degradation, content-addressable memory, generalization, and the ability to learn exception-handling patterns. PDP models have been applied to reading (triangle model: orthography-phonology-semantics), past-tense learning, face recognition, semantic cognition, and word production. They demonstrate that rule-like behavior can emerge from subsymbolic statistical learning.

**References:**
- Rumelhart, D. E., McClelland, J. L., & the PDP Research Group (1986). *Parallel Distributed Processing: Explorations in the Microstructure of Cognition*. MIT Press.
- McClelland, J. L., & Rogers, T. T. (2003). The parallel distributed processing approach to semantic cognition. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 4(4), 310–322.

---

#### 6.36 Bayesian Brain Hypothesis

The Bayesian Brain hypothesis proposes that the nervous system represents information as probability distributions and processes information according to Bayes' theorem. Perception involves combining prior beliefs with incoming sensory evidence (likelihood) to compute posterior probability distributions over latent causes. This framework explains perceptual illusions (strong priors overriding weak evidence), multisensory integration (optimal cue combination weighted by reliability), sensorimotor adaptation, and decision-making under uncertainty. It provides a normative framework (what the brain should compute) against which actual behavior can be compared. The framework has been applied to nearly every domain in cognitive neuroscience.

**References:**
- Knill, D. C., & Pouget, A. (2004). The Bayesian brain: The role of uncertainty in neural coding and computation. *Trends in Neurosciences*, 27(12), 712–719.
- Pouget, A., Beck, J. M., Ma, W. J., & Latham, P. E. (2013). Probabilistic brains: Knowns and unknowns. *Nature Neuroscience*, 16(9), 1170–1178.
- Ma, W. J., Beck, J. M., Latham, P. E., & Pouget, A. (2006). Bayesian inference with probabilistic population codes. *Nature Neuroscience*, 9(11), 1432–1438.

#### 6.37 Somatic Marker Hypothesis (Damasio)

The Somatic Marker Hypothesis proposes that decision-making is guided by emotional signals (somatic markers) arising from body states associated with the predicted outcomes of actions. When contemplating a decision, the brain reactivates physiological states (e.g., gut feelings, skin conductance changes) previously associated with similar outcomes. These somatic markers serve as rapid, intuitive signals that bias decision-making before deliberate cost-benefit analysis. Patients with ventromedial prefrontal cortex damage fail to generate somatic markers and make disadvantageous decisions despite intact intellectual abilities. The hypothesis is operationalized primarily by the Iowa Gambling Task and has been influential in neuroeconomics.

**References:**
- Damasio, A. R. (1994). *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain*. Putnam.
- Bechara, A., Damasio, H., Tranel, D., & Damasio, A. R. (2005). The Iowa Gambling Task and the somatic marker hypothesis: Some questions and answers. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 9(4), 159–162.
- Damasio, A. R., Everitt, B. J., & Bishop, D. (1996). The somatic marker hypothesis and the possible functions of the prefrontal cortex. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 351(1346), 1413–1420.

#### 6.38 Appraisal Theories of Emotion

Appraisal theories propose that emotions are elicited by cognitive evaluations (appraisals) of events along specific dimensions: relevance (does this matter?), implication (is it goal-congruent or incongruent?), coping potential (can I handle it?), and normative significance (is it fair?). Different patterns of appraisal produce different emotions (e.g., high relevance + low coping potential = fear; goal incongruence + external blame = anger). Component process models (Scherer) specify that appraisals unfold sequentially and trigger coordinated changes across motor, physiological, and experiential response systems. These theories provide a cognitive architecture for emotion that connects to emotion regulation, social cognition, and decision-making tasks.

**References:**
- Scherer, K. R. (2009). The dynamic architecture of emotion: Evidence for the component process model. *Cognition and Emotion*, 23(7), 1307–1351.
- Moors, A., Ellsworth, P. C., Scherer, K. R., & Frijda, N. H. (2013). Appraisal theories of emotion: State of the art and future development. *Emotion Review*, 5(2), 119–124.
- Roseman, I. J. (2013). Appraisal in the emotion system: Coherence in strategies for coping. *Emotion Review*, 5(2), 141–149.

#### 6.39 Network Neuroscience (Graph-Theoretic Approaches)

Network neuroscience applies graph theory to brain connectivity data (structural, functional, and effective connectivity), modeling the brain as a network of nodes (brain regions) and edges (connections). Key graph metrics include modularity (community structure), small-world properties (high clustering with short path lengths), hub/connector nodes (regions with high centrality), and dynamic reconfiguration. The framework has revealed that the brain organizes into canonical resting-state networks (default mode, frontoparietal control, dorsal attention, salience, etc.) that flexibly reconfigure during cognitive tasks. Network neuroscience provides a bridge between cognitive models (which describe processes) and neural implementation (which describes structure).

**References:**
- Bullmore, E., & Sporns, O. (2009). Complex brain networks: Graph theoretical analysis of structural and functional systems. *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 10(3), 186–198.
- Sporns, O. (2014). Contributions and challenges for network models in cognitive neuroscience. *Nature Neuroscience*, 17(5), 652–660.
- Bassett, D. S., & Sporns, O. (2017). Network neuroscience. *Nature Neuroscience*, 20(3), 353–364.

#### 6.40 Deep Learning Models of Cognition

Recent work has used deep neural networks (DNNs) as computational models of cognitive processes. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on image classification produce internal representations that correlate with representations in the ventral visual stream. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs/LSTMs) and transformers model sequential processing in language, motor control, and decision-making. These models serve as "normative" or "mechanistic" models of cognition—not claiming the brain implements backpropagation, but that the computational problems solved by DNNs and the brain are similar enough to produce convergent representations. Key contributions include modeling visual object recognition, face perception, language processing, and reinforcement learning strategies.

**References:**
- Yamins, D. L. K., & DiCarlo, J. J. (2016). Using goal-driven deep learning models to understand sensory cortex. *Nature Neuroscience*, 19(3), 356–365.
- Kriegeskorte, N. (2015). Deep neural networks: A new framework for modeling biological vision and brain information processing. *Annual Review of Vision Science*, 1, 417–446.
- Saxe, A., Nelli, S., & Summerfield, C. (2021). If deep learning is the answer, what is the question? *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 22(1), 55–67.

#### 6.41 Instance Theory of Automatization (Logan)

Instance Theory proposes that automaticity develops through the storage and retrieval of individual processing episodes (instances). In the early stages of skill learning, performance relies on general algorithmic processing. With practice, each encounter with a stimulus is stored as a separate memory trace. As traces accumulate, memory retrieval of a stored instance becomes faster than algorithmic computation, and performance transitions from algorithm-based to memory-based. The race between algorithm and memory retrieval explains the power-law speedup of practice, the transition from controlled to automatic processing, and individual differences in automatization rate. The theory applies to tasks like Stroop (where word reading is automatic due to extensive practice) and visual search.

**References:**
- Logan, G. D. (1988). Toward an instance theory of automatization. *Psychological Review*, 95(4), 492–527.
- Logan, G. D. (2002). An instance theory of attention and memory. *Psychological Review*, 109(2), 376–400.

#### 6.42 Embodied Cognition Framework

Embodied cognition proposes that cognitive processes are deeply rooted in the body's sensorimotor interactions with the environment. Abstract concepts are grounded in sensory and motor simulations—understanding "grasp" activates motor cortex regions involved in grasping. The framework challenges the classical view that cognition operates on amodal, abstract symbols and instead proposes that perception, action, and cognition share representational resources. Evidence includes the motor-compatibility effect, spatial-numerical associations (SNARC effect), and interference between physical and conceptual actions. Applied to language, embodied models predict that processing action verbs activates motor cortex.

**References:**
- Barsalou, L. W. (2008). Grounded cognition. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 59, 617–645.
- Wilson, M. (2002). Six views of embodied cognition. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 9(4), 625–636.
- Gallese, V., & Lakoff, G. (2005). The brain's concepts: The role of the sensory-motor system in conceptual knowledge. *Cognitive Neuropsychology*, 22(3-4), 455–479.

#### 6.43 Active Inference

Active Inference extends the Free Energy Principle (Section 6.9) from perception to action: agents minimize surprise (free energy) not only by updating beliefs (perception) but also by acting on the world to bring about expected sensory states. Behavior is driven by prior preferences (goals encoded as expected observations) and epistemic value (reducing uncertainty). The framework unifies perception, action, decision-making, and learning under a single principle—expected free energy minimization. It provides a process theory for planning, habit formation, and exploration-exploitation tradeoffs. Active inference has been applied to modeling eye movements, motor control, decision-making in volatility, and Pavlovian/instrumental conditioning tasks.

**References:**
- Friston, K. J., FitzGerald, T., Rigoli, F., Schwartenbeck, P., & Pezzulo, G. (2017). Active inference: A process theory. *Neural Computation*, 29(1), 1–49.
- Parr, T., Pezzulo, G., & Friston, K. J. (2022). *Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior*. MIT Press.
- Da Costa, L., Parr, T., Sajid, N., Vesber, S., Ryan, V., & Friston, K. (2020). Active inference on discrete state-spaces: A synthesis. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology*, 99, 102447.
