## 5.1 Perception and Perceptual Decision-Making

---

### Task 79: Random Dot Kinematogram (Dot Motion Task)

The random dot kinematogram presents a field of moving dots in which a variable proportion move coherently in one direction while the remainder move randomly. Participants judge the direction of coherent motion, and the proportion of coherently moving dots parametrically controls task difficulty. This paradigm is the foundational tool for studying perceptual decision-making and has been central to establishing the neural basis of evidence accumulation, linking single-neuron recordings in area MT/V5 and LIP to drift-diffusion models of choice. It bridges sensory processing and decision-making in a way no other paradigm does as cleanly.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Perceptual evidence accumulation and drift rate
- Speed-accuracy tradeoff in decision-making
- Motion perception and global motion integration
- Response threshold and decision boundary setting
- Sensory noise tolerance and signal detection
- Top-down attentional modulation of sensory evidence

**Key References:**
1. Britten, K. H., Shadlen, M. N., Newsome, W. T., & Movshon, J. A. (1992). The analysis of visual motion: A comparison of neuronal and psychophysical performance. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 12(12), 4745–4765.
2. Shadlen, M. N., & Newsome, W. T. (2001). Neural basis of a perceptual decision in the parietal cortex (area LIP) of the rhesus monkey. *Journal of Neurophysiology*, 86(4), 1916–1936.
3. Gold, J. I., & Shadlen, M. N. (2007). The neural basis of decision making. *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 30, 535–574.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Ratcliff, R., Smith, P. L., Brown, S. D., & McKoon, G. (2016). Diffusion decision model: Current issues and history. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 20(4), 260–281.
2. Shooshtari, S. V., Sadrabadi, J. E., Azizi, Z., & Ebrahimpour, R. (2019). Confidence representation of perceptual decision by EEG and eye data in a random dot motion task. *Neuroscience*, 406, 510–527.
3. Steinemann, N. A., O'Connell, R. G., & Kelly, S. P. (2018). Decisions are expedited through multiple neural adjustments spanning the sensorimotor hierarchy. *Nature Communications*, 9, 3627.
4. Turner, B. M., van Maanen, L., Forstmann, B. U., & Brown, S. D. (2018). Approaches to analysis in model-based cognitive neuroscience. *Journal of Mathematical Psychology*, 76, 65–79.

**Task Variations:**
- **Two-Alternative Forced Choice (Standard)**: Leftward vs. rightward coherent motion; the canonical version used in monkey neurophysiology.
- **Variable Coherence Levels**: Parametric manipulation from 0% to 100% coherence to trace psychometric and chronometric functions.
- **Free Response vs. Interrogation Protocol**: Participant-initiated response vs. experimenter-controlled viewing duration; separates speed-accuracy tradeoff from evidence quality.
- **Pulse Paradigm**: Brief motion pulses embedded in noise to measure the time course of evidence accumulation.
- **Motion Energy Reverse Correlation**: Using random fluctuations in motion energy to reconstruct the temporal weighting of evidence (psychophysical kernel analysis).
- **Multi-Alternative Motion Discrimination**: Four or more possible directions; tests extensions of drift-diffusion to multi-choice scenarios.
- **Confidence Judgment Version**: Adding post-decision confidence ratings to study metacognition of perceptual decisions.
- **Speed-Accuracy Emphasis Manipulation**: Instructions or cues emphasizing speed vs. accuracy to manipulate response thresholds.
- **Reward-Biased Motion Discrimination**: Asymmetric rewards for correct responses to different directions; measures prior probability effects on evidence accumulation.
- **Motion Discrimination with EEG**: Adding EEG to track centro-parietal positivity (CPP) as a neural correlate of evidence accumulation.
- **Gradual vs. Abrupt Onset**: Coherent motion emerges gradually or appears suddenly; tests integration onset dynamics.

### Task 80: Biological Motion (Point-Light Walker) Task

Participants view point-light displays — animations in which only the major joints of a human figure are visible as moving dots against a dark background — and identify whether the display depicts a human action, discriminate between actions, or detect the presence of biological motion embedded in noise dots. The paradigm demonstrates the visual system's remarkable sensitivity to the kinematics of biological agents, recruiting the superior temporal sulcus and fusiform regions. It is a standard tool in social perception, action understanding, developmental psychology, and clinical research on autism spectrum disorder.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Biological motion perception and configural processing
- Action recognition from kinematic information
- Social perception and agent detection
- Form-from-motion extraction
- Animacy perception and intentionality attribution
- Visual temporal integration of body kinematics

**Key References:**
1. Johansson, G. (1973). Visual perception of biological motion and a model for its analysis. *Perception & Psychophysics*, 14(2), 201–211.
2. Blake, R., & Shiffrar, M. (2007). Perception of human motion. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 58, 47–73.
3. Grossman, E. D., & Blake, R. (2002). Brain areas active during visual perception of biological motion. *Neuron*, 35(6), 1167–1175.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Pavlova, M. A. (2012). Biological motion processing as a hallmark of social cognition. *Cerebral Cortex*, 22(5), 981–995.
2. Thurman, S. M., & Lu, H. (2016). Revisiting the importance of common body dynamics in human action perception. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 78, 2187–2199.
3. van Boxtel, J. J. A., & Lu, H. (2013). A biological motion toolbox for reading, displaying, and manipulating motion capture data in research settings. *Journal of Vision*, 13(12), 7.
4. Miller, L. E., & Saygin, A. P. (2013). Individual differences in the perception of biological motion: Links to social cognition and motor imagery. *Cognition*, 128(2), 140–148.

**Task Variations:**
- **Point-Light Walker Detection in Noise**: Biological motion walker embedded in scrambled-motion noise dots; measure detection threshold.
- **Action Identification**: Identify what action the point-light figure is performing (walking, running, dancing, throwing).
- **Upright vs. Inverted**: Inversion effect — inverting the display disrupts perception, demonstrating configural processing.
- **Facing Direction Discrimination**: Determine whether the walker faces left or right; sensitive to viewpoint processing.
- **Emotion from Point-Light Motion**: Judge emotional state (happy, sad, angry) conveyed by body kinematics alone.
- **Gender Discrimination from Gait**: Judge walker sex from kinematic cues alone.
- **Scrambled vs. Intact Comparison**: Scrambled displays (dots repositioned but retaining individual joint trajectories) vs. intact; isolates configural from local motion cues.
- **Biological vs. Non-Biological Motion**: Discriminate human point-light motion from mechanical or random motion patterns.
- **Dynamic Occlusion**: Portions of the point-light figure are occluded; measures tolerance of the visual system to incomplete biological motion input.
- **EEG/fMRI Versions with STS Localization**: Neuroimaging combined with the task to isolate superior temporal sulcus activation.

### Task 81: Global-Local (Navon) Task

A large letter (the global level) is composed of smaller letters (the local level) — for instance, a large "H" made of small "S" characters. Participants identify the letter at either the global or local level, and the two levels can be congruent (same letter) or incongruent (different letters). Navon (1977) demonstrated a global precedence effect: faster identification of global than local targets, and asymmetric interference where global information disrupts local processing more than the reverse. The paradigm probes hierarchical visual processing, hemispheric lateralization, and attentional scope, and is widely used in clinical research on autism, ADHD, and hemispatial neglect.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Global vs. local processing and hierarchical perception
- Global precedence and attentional scope
- Interference between hierarchical levels
- Hemispheric specialization for spatial frequency
- Attentional flexibility and processing level selection
- Configural vs. featural processing strategies

**Key References:**
1. Navon, D. (1977). Forest before trees: The precedence of global features in visual perception. *Cognitive Psychology*, 9(3), 353–383.
2. Kimchi, R. (1992). Primacy of wholistic processing and global/local paradigm: A critical review. *Psychological Bulletin*, 112(1), 24–38.
3. Fink, G. R., Halligan, P. W., Marshall, J. C., Frith, C. D., Frackowiak, R. S. J., & Dolan, R. J. (1996). Where in the brain does visual attention select the forest and the trees? *Nature*, 382, 626–628.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Poirel, N., Pineau, A., & Mellet, E. (2008). What does the nature of the stimuli tell us about the Global Precedence Effect? *Acta Psychologica*, 127(1), 1–11.
2. Kaur, J., & Paul, S. (2022). Global precedence changes by environment: A systematic review and meta-analysis on effect of perceptual field variables on global-local visual processing. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 84, 1833–1877.
3. Song, Y., Hakoda, Y., & Sang, B. (2015). Lack of global precedence and global-to-local interference without local processing deficit in children with ADHD. *Journal of Attention Disorders*, 20(8), 671–682.
4. Dale, G., & Bhattacharjee, D. (2023). Global and local processing of letters and faces: The role of visual focal attention. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 14, 1114021.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Navon Letter Task**: Identify global or local letter in a blocked or mixed design.
- **Divided Attention Version**: Respond to targets at either level on every trial; measures automatic processing of both levels.
- **Selective Attention Version**: Attend to one level while ignoring the other; measures interference from the unattended level.
- **Congruent vs. Incongruent Conditions**: Same vs. different identity at global and local levels; measures cross-level interference.
- **Sparse vs. Dense Element Spacing**: Varying the number and spacing of local elements; affects the global-local balance.
- **Navon Figures with Shapes**: Geometric shapes rather than letters; extends the paradigm beyond verbal stimuli.
- **Switching Between Levels**: Alternating between global and local attention across trials; measures switch cost.
- **Mood Induction + Global-Local**: Positive affect broadens attentional scope (more global processing); negative affect narrows it.
- **Hemisfield Presentation**: Left vs. right visual field presentation; tests hemispheric specialization (right hemisphere → global, left hemisphere → local).
- **Temporal Precedence (Brief Exposure)**: Very brief presentations (50–100 ms) to measure the time course of global vs. local availability.

## 5.2 Memory Control and Metamemory

---

### Task 82: Remember/Know Paradigm

During a recognition memory test, participants classify each item they judge as "old" according to the subjective quality of their memory: "remember" if they can recollect specific contextual details from the encoding episode (e.g., what they were thinking, where the item appeared), or "know" if the item feels familiar but retrieval of contextual details fails. Tulving (1985) introduced this distinction to operationalize the difference between episodic recollection and noetic familiarity. The paradigm has become the primary behavioral tool for dual-process theories of recognition memory and has been extensively validated with neuroimaging, revealing dissociable hippocampal (recollection) and perirhinal (familiarity) contributions.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Recollection vs. familiarity in recognition memory
- Episodic memory and autonoetic consciousness
- Source memory and contextual retrieval
- Dual-process models of recognition
- Metamemory and subjective experience of remembering
- Hippocampal vs. perirhinal cortex memory systems

**Key References:**
1. Tulving, E. (1985). Memory and consciousness. *Canadian Psychology*, 26(1), 1–12.
2. Yonelinas, A. P. (2002). The nature of recollection and familiarity: A review of 30 years of research. *Journal of Memory and Language*, 46(3), 441–517.
3. Gardiner, J. M. (1988). Functional aspects of recollective experience. *Memory & Cognition*, 16(4), 309–313.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Yonelinas, A. P., Aly, M., Wang, W.-C., & Koen, J. D. (2010). Recollection and familiarity: Examining controversial assumptions and new directions. *Hippocampus*, 20(11), 1178–1194.
2. Wixted, J. T., & Mickes, L. (2010). A continuous dual-process model of remember/know judgments. *Psychological Review*, 117(4), 1025–1054.
3. Migo, E. M., Mayes, A. R., & Montaldi, D. (2012). Measuring recollection and familiarity: Improving the remember/know procedure. *Consciousness and Cognition*, 21(3), 1435–1455.
4. Bastin, C., Besson, G., Simon, J., Delhaye, E., Geurten, M., Willems, S., & Salmon, E. (2019). An integrative memory model of recollection and familiarity to understand memory deficits. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 42, e281.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Remember/Know**: Binary R/K judgment on each recognized item; the canonical version.
- **Remember/Know/Guess (RKG)**: Three-way classification adding "guess" to capture below-threshold familiarity.
- **Remember/Know with Source Memory**: R/K judgment followed by a source question (e.g., which list, which voice, which location).
- **Remember/Know with Confidence Ratings**: Combining R/K with graded confidence to distinguish dual-process from signal-detection accounts.
- **Associative Remember/Know**: Applied to associative recognition (word pairs) rather than item recognition.
- **Encoding Manipulation Versions**: Levels-of-processing, divided attention, or emotional encoding manipulations followed by R/K test.
- **R/K in Aging and Amnesia**: Applied to patient populations; familiarity may be preserved when recollection is impaired.
- **Event-Related fMRI Remember/Know**: Sorting recognition trials by R/K response to isolate hippocampal recollection signals.
- **R/K with Process Dissociation**: Combining R/K with Jacoby's PDP for converging estimates of recollection and familiarity.
- **Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) Analysis**: R/K combined with confidence to generate ROC curves distinguishing recollection from familiarity.

### Task 83: Directed Forgetting Task

Participants study items (typically words) under instructions that each item should either be remembered or forgotten. In the item method, a remember or forget cue follows each individual item. In the list method, a single cue midway through the study phase instructs participants to forget everything studied so far. On a subsequent memory test, recall and recognition of forget-cued items is impaired relative to remember-cued items. The paradigm provides the primary laboratory tool for studying intentional forgetting and memory control, with theoretical implications for understanding inhibitory control over memory, context change, and selective rehearsal.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Intentional forgetting and memory control
- Inhibitory control over memory retrieval
- Selective rehearsal and encoding regulation
- Context change and mental set shifting
- Proactive interference resolution
- Retrieval inhibition mechanisms

**Key References:**
1. Bjork, R. A. (1970). Positive forgetting: The noninterference of items intentionally forgotten. *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior*, 9(3), 255–268.
2. MacLeod, C. M. (1998). Directed forgetting. In J. M. Golding & C. M. MacLeod (Eds.), *Intentional forgetting: Interdisciplinary approaches* (pp. 1–57). Erlbaum.
3. Bjork, R. A. (1989). Retrieval inhibition as an adaptive mechanism in human memory. In H. L. Roediger III & F. I. M. Craik (Eds.), *Varieties of memory and consciousness: Essays in honour of Endel Tulving* (pp. 309–330). Erlbaum.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Anderson, M. C., & Hanslmayr, S. (2014). Neural mechanisms of motivated forgetting. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 18(6), 279–292.
2. Pastötter, B., & Bäuml, K.-H. T. (2014). Distinct slow and fast cortical theta dynamics in episodic memory retrieval. *NeuroImage*, 94, 155–161.
3. Sahakyan, L., Delaney, P. F., Foster, N. L., & Abushanab, B. (2013). List-method directed forgetting in cognitive and clinical research: A theoretical and methodological review. *Psychology of Learning and Motivation*, 59, 131–189.
4. Fellner, M.-C., Waldhauser, G. T., & Axmacher, N. (2020). Tracking selective rehearsal and active inhibition of memory traces in directed forgetting. *Current Biology*, 30(13), 2638–2644.

**Task Variations:**
- **Item-Method Directed Forgetting**: Remember/forget cue after each individual item; primarily affects encoding.
- **List-Method Directed Forgetting**: Single forget cue after the first list; affects both retrieval and encoding of second list.
- **Recognition vs. Recall Test**: Directed forgetting effects are typically larger in recall than recognition.
- **Directed Forgetting with Source Memory**: Testing whether source information is forgotten alongside item information.
- **Emotional Directed Forgetting**: Emotional vs. neutral items; emotional items may resist directed forgetting.
- **Directed Forgetting with ERP**: N2 and P3 components index the inhibitory processes following forget cues.
- **Think/No-Think Comparison**: Pairing directed forgetting with the TNT task to compare encoding-based vs. retrieval-based forgetting mechanisms.
- **Clinical Applications (PTSD, Depression)**: Testing whether intrusive-memory populations show impaired directed forgetting.
- **Directed Forgetting of Actions (SPT)**: Forgetting subject-performed tasks; tests embodied memory control.
- **Cumulative Directed Forgetting**: Multiple lists with forget cues; tests whether inhibition accumulates across lists.

### Task 84: Think/No-Think Task

Participants first learn cue-target word pairs (e.g., "ordeal–roach"). In the critical phase, cues are presented and participants are instructed either to recall the target (Think) or to prevent the target from coming to mind (No-Think). On a final test, suppressed (No-Think) items show reduced recall relative to baseline items that were learned but not presented during the critical phase. Anderson and Green (2001) introduced this paradigm as a memory analog of the Go/No-Go motor inhibition task, arguing that just as people can stop a motor action, they can suppress retrieval of unwanted memories. The paradigm has become central to research on memory suppression, PTSD, and the role of prefrontal control over hippocampal memory retrieval.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Retrieval suppression and memory inhibition
- Prefrontal executive control over hippocampal retrieval
- Intrusive memory management
- Suppression-induced forgetting
- Voluntary control of memory access
- Relationship between motor inhibition and memory inhibition

**Key References:**
1. Anderson, M. C., & Green, C. (2001). Suppressing unwanted memories by executive control. *Nature*, 410, 366–369.
2. Anderson, M. C., & Hanslmayr, S. (2014). Neural mechanisms of motivated forgetting. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 18(6), 279–292.
3. Levy, B. J., & Anderson, M. C. (2012). Purging of memories from conscious awareness tracked in the human brain. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 32(47), 16785–16794.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Gagnepain, P., Hulbert, J., & Anderson, M. C. (2017). Parallel regulation of memory and emotion supports the suppression of intrusive memories. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 37(27), 6423–6441.
2. Benoit, R. G., & Anderson, M. C. (2012). Opposing mechanisms support the voluntary forgetting of unwanted memories. *Neuron*, 76(2), 450–460.
3. Stramaccia, D. F., Meyer, A.-K., Rischer, K. M., Fawcett, J. M., & Benoit, R. G. (2021). Memory suppression and its deficiency in psychological disorders: A focused review. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 150(5), 828–850.
4. Hu, X., Bergström, Z. M., Gagnepain, P., & Anderson, M. C. (2017). Suppressing unwanted memories reduces their unintended influences. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 26(2), 197–206.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Think/No-Think (TNT)**: Word-pair learning followed by think/no-think phase and final cued recall; the Anderson & Green (2001) version.
- **Independent Probe Test**: Testing suppressed items with novel, category cues rather than the learned cue; demonstrates cue-independent forgetting indicative of inhibition.
- **TNT with Intrusion Reporting**: Participants report whether the target came to mind despite suppression attempts; tracks suppression success over repetitions.
- **Emotional TNT**: Negative or trauma-related word pairs or scene-face pairs; tests suppression of emotional memories.
- **TNT with fMRI**: Tracks DLPFC engagement and hippocampal down-regulation during No-Think trials.
- **TNT with EEG**: Theta power and P3 amplitude index suppression effort.
- **TNT with Valence Manipulation**: Pairing neutral cues with negative targets; particularly relevant to PTSD models.
- **TNT in Clinical Populations**: Testing patients with PTSD, depression, or anxiety to assess suppression ability.
- **TNT with Thought Substitution**: Instructing participants to replace the unwanted memory with an alternative thought; contrasts direct suppression with thought substitution.
- **Dose-Response TNT**: Varying the number of suppression attempts (0, 2, 4, 8, 12, 16) to trace the dose-response curve of suppression.

## 5.3 Decision-Making and Reinforcement Learning

---

### Task 85: Multi-Armed Bandit Task

Participants choose among multiple options (the "arms" of a slot machine), each yielding rewards with unknown and often changing probabilities. The central challenge is the explore-exploit dilemma: whether to exploit the currently best-known option or explore alternatives that might yield higher returns. Reward probabilities may be stationary or volatile (drifting over time), with the volatile version (restless bandit) requiring continuous updating of value estimates. This paradigm is the dominant tool in computational psychiatry for studying reinforcement learning, and its computational tractability — fitting with Bayesian, Kalman-filter, or upper-confidence-bound models — has made it central to understanding decision-making deficits in addiction, schizophrenia, depression, and anxiety.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Explore-exploit tradeoff in decision-making
- Reinforcement learning and value updating
- Uncertainty-driven exploration vs. random exploration
- Belief updating under volatility
- Reward sensitivity and learning rate
- Computational model parameters (learning rate, inverse temperature, exploration bonus)

**Key References:**
1. Daw, N. D., O'Doherty, J. P., Dayan, P., Seymour, B., & Dolan, R. J. (2006). Cortical substrates for exploratory decisions in humans. *Nature*, 441, 876–879.
2. Cohen, J. D., McClure, S. M., & Yu, A. J. (2007). Should I stay or should I go? How the human brain manages the trade-off between exploitation and exploration. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 362(1481), 933–942.
3. Sutton, R. S., & Barto, A. G. (2018). *Reinforcement learning: An introduction* (2nd ed.). MIT Press.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Gershman, S. J. (2018). Deconstructing the human algorithms for exploration. *Cognition*, 173, 34–42.
2. Schulz, E., & Gershman, S. J. (2019). The algorithmic architecture of exploration in the human brain. *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 55, 7–14.
3. Cogliati Dezza, I., Yu, A. J., Cleeremans, A., & Alexander, W. (2017). Learning the value of information and reward over time when solving exploration–exploitation problems. *Scientific Reports*, 7, 16919.
4. Chakroun, K., Mathar, D., Wiehler, A., Ganzer, F., & Peters, J. (2020). Dopaminergic modulation of the exploration/exploitation trade-off in human decision-making. *eLife*, 9, e51260.

**Task Variations:**
- **Two-Armed Stationary Bandit**: Simplest version; two options with fixed reward probabilities.
- **Restless (Volatile) Bandit**: Reward probabilities drift over time via Gaussian random walk; requires continuous updating.
- **Contextual Bandit**: Reward probabilities depend on observable context features; tests generalization across contexts.
- **Horizon Task (Wilson et al.)**: Short vs. long decision horizons to separately measure directed and random exploration.
- **Four-Armed Bandit with Reversal**: Multiple arms with occasional reward-probability reversals; combines bandit with reversal-learning demands.
- **Informative vs. Non-Informative Exploration**: Designs that separate information-seeking exploration from random exploration (e.g., observed vs. chosen options).
- **Social Bandit**: Observing another agent's choices and outcomes before making own decisions; adds social learning dimension.
- **Bandit with Effort Cost**: Incorporating physical or cognitive effort cost into exploration decisions.
- **Bandit with Partial Observability**: Only the chosen arm's outcome is observed (standard) vs. all arms' outcomes are observed; separates learning from exploration.
- **Bayesian vs. Heuristic Model Fitting**: Same task data analyzed with UCB, Thompson sampling, softmax, or epsilon-greedy models to characterize individual exploration strategies.

### Task 86: Two-Stage Decision Task

Participants make two sequential choices per trial. The first-stage choice leads (with fixed transition probabilities — 70% common, 30% rare) to one of two second-stage states, each containing its own pair of options. Second-stage options yield rewards with slowly drifting probabilities. Daw et al. (2011) designed this task to dissociate model-based reinforcement learning (using knowledge of the transition structure to plan) from model-free reinforcement learning (repeating previously rewarded actions regardless of transition type). The diagnostic signature is the interaction between reward and transition type on subsequent first-stage choices: model-based agents show opposite stay/switch patterns after common vs. rare transitions, while model-free agents show the same pattern regardless of transition type.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Model-based vs. model-free reinforcement learning
- Planning and forward-looking decision-making
- Habitual vs. goal-directed action control
- Transition-structure learning
- Reward prediction error computation
- Executive control over decision strategies

**Key References:**
1. Daw, N. D., Gershman, S. J., Seymour, B., Dayan, P., & Dolan, R. J. (2011). Model-based influences on humans' choices and striatal prediction errors. *Neuron*, 69(6), 1204–1215.
2. Dolan, R. J., & Dayan, P. (2013). Goals and habits in the brain. *Neuron*, 80(2), 312–325.
3. Glascher, J., Daw, N., Dayan, P., & O'Doherty, J. P. (2010). States versus rewards: Dissociable neural prediction error signals underlying model-based and model-free reinforcement learning. *Neuron*, 66(4), 585–595.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Kool, W., Cushman, F. A., & Gershman, S. J. (2016). When does model-based control pay off? *PLoS Computational Biology*, 12(8), e1005090.
2. Gillan, C. M., Kosinski, M., Whelan, R., Phelps, E. A., & Daw, N. D. (2016). Characterizing a psychiatric symptom dimension related to deficits in goal-directed control. *eLife*, 5, e11305.
3. da Silva, C. F., & Hare, T. A. (2020). Humans primarily use model-based inference in the two-stage task. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 4, 1053–1066.
4. Feher da Silva, C., & Hare, T. A. (2018). A note on the analysis of two-stage task results: How changes in task structure affect what model-free and model-based strategies predict about the effects of reward and transition on the stay probability. *PLoS ONE*, 13(4), e0195328.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Daw et al. (2011) Version**: Two first-stage options, two second-stage states, each with two options; 70/30 transition probabilities.
- **Shortened/Simplified Versions**: Fewer trials or simplified transition structure for clinical or developmental populations.
- **Enhanced Model-Based Version**: More complex transition structures (three or more stages) to increase model-based demands.
- **Devaluation Manipulation**: Changing reward magnitudes mid-task to test sensitivity to outcome value (model-based predicts rapid adjustment).
- **fMRI Version**: Imaging during the task to identify striatal prediction-error signals and prefrontal planning signals.
- **Two-Stage with Instructed Knowledge**: Explicitly teaching the transition structure before the task; tests whether model-based behavior increases with explicit knowledge.
- **Clinical Applications (Addiction, OCD, Schizophrenia)**: Reduced model-based control in compulsive and habitual disorders.
- **Two-Stage with Working Memory Load**: Concurrent WM load to test whether model-based control depends on executive resources.
- **Adolescent / Developmental Versions**: Tracking the emergence of model-based control across development.
- **Online/Large-Sample Versions**: Web-based implementations for large-N individual-difference studies linking model-based control to psychiatric symptoms.

### Task 87: Implicit Association Test (IAT)

Participants rapidly classify stimuli from four categories using two response keys. Two categories are target concepts (e.g., "flowers" vs. "insects") and two are evaluative attributes (e.g., "pleasant" vs. "unpleasant"). In the critical comparison, one block pairs congruent categories on the same key (flowers + pleasant) and another pairs incongruent categories (flowers + unpleasant). The IAT effect — faster responding in the congruent block — is interpreted as reflecting the strength of automatic associations between the concepts and attributes. Greenwald et al. (1998) introduced the IAT as a measure of implicit attitudes, and it has become one of the most widely administered psychological tests, with applications spanning racial bias, self-esteem, clinical anxiety, consumer preferences, and political attitudes.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Implicit attitudes and automatic associations
- Evaluative categorization and response compatibility
- Dissociation between explicit and implicit cognition
- Automatic stereotype activation
- Response competition and task-set reconfiguration
- Individual differences in implicit social cognition

**Key References:**
1. Greenwald, A. G., McGhee, D. E., & Schwartz, J. L. K. (1998). Measuring individual differences in implicit cognition: The Implicit Association Test. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 74(6), 1464–1480.
2. Greenwald, A. G., Nosek, B. A., & Banaji, M. R. (2003). Understanding and using the Implicit Association Test: I. An improved scoring algorithm. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 85(2), 197–216.
3. Nosek, B. A., Greenwald, A. G., & Banaji, M. R. (2005). Understanding and using the Implicit Association Test: II. Method variables and construct validity. *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 31(2), 166–180.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Greenwald, A. G., Banaji, M. R., & Nosek, B. A. (2015). Statistically small effects of the Implicit Association Test can have societally large effects. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 108(4), 553–561.
2. Kurdi, B., Seitchik, A. E., Axt, J. R., Carroll, T. J., Karapetyan, A., Kaushik, N., ... & Banaji, M. R. (2019). Relationship between the Implicit Association Test and intergroup behavior: A meta-analysis. *American Psychologist*, 74(5), 569–586.
3. Meissner, F., Grigutsch, L. A., Koranyi, N., Müller, F., & Rothermund, K. (2019). Predicting behavior with implicit measures: Disillusioning findings, reasonable explanations, and sophisticated solutions. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 10, 2483.
4. Charlesworth, T. E. S., & Banaji, M. R. (2019). Patterns of implicit and explicit attitudes: I. Long-term change and stability from 2007 to 2016. *Psychological Science*, 30(2), 174–192.

**Task Variations:**
- **Race IAT**: Black/White faces paired with pleasant/unpleasant words; the most studied version.
- **Gender-Science IAT**: Male/female paired with science/liberal arts; measures implicit gender-career stereotypes.
- **Self-Esteem IAT**: Self/other paired with pleasant/unpleasant; measures implicit self-evaluation.
- **Single-Category IAT (SC-IAT)**: Only one target category; measures absolute rather than relative associations.
- **Brief IAT (BIAT)**: Shortened version with fewer trials; suited for battery administration.
- **Go/No-Go Association Task (GNAT)**: Related implicit measure using go/no-go responses rather than two-choice classification.
- **Recoding-Free IAT (IAT-RF)**: Eliminates recoding confound by using four response keys.
- **Personalized IAT (P-IAT)**: Uses "I like" / "I don't like" labels instead of "pleasant" / "unpleasant"; reduces normative responding.
- **IAT with EEG/fMRI**: Neuroimaging to identify conflict-related (N2, ACC) and evaluative processing during the task.
- **Developmental / Child IAT**: Simplified versions for children using pictures rather than words.

## 5.4 Reasoning and Intelligence

---

### Task 88: Raven's Progressive Matrices

Participants view a matrix pattern with a missing element and select the correct completion from a set of alternatives. The patterns involve systematic variations in features such as shape, size, color, orientation, and number, governed by rules that increase in complexity across test items. Solving each item requires extracting the governing rules and applying them to predict the missing element. Raven's Progressive Matrices is the most widely used culture-reduced measure of fluid intelligence (Gf) and abstract relational reasoning, and is among the most extensively studied psychometric instruments in all of psychology. It has been central to debates about the neural basis of general intelligence, g-loading, and the structure of reasoning.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Fluid intelligence and abstract reasoning
- Relational reasoning and rule induction
- Analogical thinking and pattern completion
- Working memory capacity as it supports reasoning
- Visuospatial problem solving
- Goal management and subgoal decomposition

**Key References:**
1. Raven, J. C. (1938). *Progressive Matrices: A perceptual test of intelligence*. H.K. Lewis.
2. Carpenter, P. A., Just, M. A., & Shell, P. (1990). What one intelligence test measures: A theoretical account of the processing in the Raven Progressive Matrices Test. *Psychological Review*, 97(3), 404–431.
3. Gray, J. R., Chabris, C. F., & Braver, T. S. (2003). Neural mechanisms of general fluid intelligence. *Nature Neuroscience*, 6(3), 316–322.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Prabhakaran, V., Smith, J. A. L., Desmond, J. E., Glover, G. H., & Gabrieli, J. D. E. (1997). Neural substrates of fluid reasoning: An fMRI study of neocortical activation during performance of the Raven's Progressive Matrices test. *Cognitive Psychology*, 33(1), 43–63.
2. Kievit, R. A., Davis, S. W., Griffiths, J., Correia, M. M., Cam-CAN, & Henson, R. N. (2016). A watershed model of individual differences in fluid intelligence. *Neuropsychologia*, 91, 186–198.
3. Matzen, L. E., Benz, Z. O., Dixon, K. R., Posey, J., Kroger, J. K., & Speed, A. E. (2010). Recreating Raven's: Software for systematically generating large numbers of Raven-like matrix problems with normed properties. *Behavior Research Methods*, 42(2), 525–541.
4. Hayes, T. R., Petrov, A. A., & Sederberg, P. B. (2015). Do we really become smarter when our fluid-intelligence test scores improve? *Intelligence*, 48, 1–14.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Progressive Matrices (SPM)**: 60 items in 5 sets (A–E) of increasing difficulty; the most widely used version.
- **Advanced Progressive Matrices (APM)**: 36 more difficult items for high-ability adults; used in cognitive neuroscience research.
- **Colored Progressive Matrices (CPM)**: Easier version for children (aged 5–11), the elderly, and clinical populations.
- **Computerized Adaptive Version**: Item difficulty adjusted in real time based on performance.
- **Matrix Reasoning (WAIS subtest)**: Similar principle but part of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale; brief screener format.
- **Sandia Matrices**: Algorithmically generated Raven's-like items with controlled rule complexity for experimental manipulation.
- **Time-Limited vs. Untimed Administration**: Power test (untimed) vs. speeded versions; affects what the test measures.
- **fMRI Raven's**: Imaging during matrix completion to identify frontoparietal network activation.
- **Rule-Complexity Manipulation**: Systematically varying the number of rules and rule types (distribution-of-three, quantitative progression, figure addition) to decompose reasoning demands.
- **Process-Tracing Versions**: Eye-tracking or think-aloud protocols during matrix completion to study strategy use.

## 5.5 Implicit Learning

---

### Task 89: Artificial Grammar Learning Task

Participants are first exposed to letter strings (e.g., "MXRVXT," "VXTTRM") generated by a finite-state grammar, often under the cover story that they are memorizing the strings. In a subsequent test phase, they classify novel letter strings as grammatical or non-grammatical, performing above chance despite being unable to articulate the underlying rules. Reber (1967) introduced this paradigm as the first laboratory demonstration of implicit learning — the acquisition of complex, rule-governed knowledge without conscious awareness. The paradigm has been central to debates about the nature of implicit vs. explicit knowledge, the role of consciousness in learning, and the neural systems supporting rule abstraction.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Implicit learning and unconscious rule abstraction
- Statistical learning and chunk frequency sensitivity
- Explicit vs. implicit knowledge dissociation
- Transfer of abstract structure across surface forms
- Sequence learning and temporal regularity extraction
- Analogy to natural language acquisition processes

**Key References:**
1. Reber, A. S. (1967). Implicit learning of artificial grammars. *Journal of Verbal Learning and Verbal Behavior*, 6(6), 855–863.
2. Reber, A. S. (1989). Implicit learning and tacit knowledge. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 118(3), 219–235.
3. Knowlton, B. J., & Squire, L. R. (1996). Artificial grammar learning depends on implicit acquisition of both abstract and exemplar-specific information. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 22(1), 169–181.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Pothos, E. M. (2007). Theories of artificial grammar learning. *Psychological Bulletin*, 133(2), 227–244.
2. Forkstam, C., & Petersson, K. M. (2005). Towards an explicit account of implicit learning. *Current Opinion in Neurology*, 18(4), 435–441.
3. Rohrmeier, M. A., & Cross, I. (2014). Modelling unsupervised online-learning of artificial grammars: Linking implicit and statistical learning. *Consciousness and Cognition*, 27, 155–167.
4. Batterink, L. J., Reber, P. J., Neville, H. J., & Paller, K. A. (2015). Implicit and explicit contributions to statistical learning. *Journal of Memory and Language*, 83, 62–78.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard AGL (Reber)**: Exposure to grammatical strings followed by grammaticality judgment of novel strings.
- **Transfer Version**: Training on strings from one letter set, testing on strings from a different letter set that preserves the abstract grammar; tests rule abstraction vs. surface-feature learning.
- **Chunk Strength Control**: Equating the frequency of letter bigrams and trigrams between grammatical and non-grammatical test items to rule out fragment-based classification.
- **Production Task**: Participants generate strings they believe are grammatical rather than classifying; tests the nature of acquired knowledge.
- **Sequential AGL**: Presenting strings one letter at a time to study online prediction and temporal learning.
- **AGL with Neuroimaging**: fMRI reveals Broca's area involvement, linking AGL to language processing circuitry.
- **Hierarchical AGL**: Grammars with center-embedded or hierarchical structure; tests whether implicit learning extends to recursive rules.
- **AGL in Amnesia**: Testing amnesic patients to demonstrate basal-ganglia-dependent implicit learning when hippocampal explicit learning is impaired.
- **AGL with Confidence Ratings**: Adding confidence judgments to test whether accurate classification occurs at low confidence (implicit knowledge signature).
- **Cross-Modal AGL**: Auditory or tactile sequences governed by the same grammar; tests modality-independence of implicit learning.

### Task 90: Contextual Cueing Task

Participants perform visual search for a T-shaped target among L-shaped distractors. Critically, half the search displays repeat across blocks (with the target always in the same location within a given repeated display), while the other half are newly generated on each block. Search becomes faster for repeated displays over blocks, even though participants cannot consciously recognize which displays have been repeated. Chun and Jiang (1998) demonstrated that this contextual cueing effect reflects implicit learning and memory for spatial configurations that guide spatial attention to the likely target location. The paradigm has become a standard tool for studying the interface between attention, implicit learning, and long-term memory.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Implicit spatial learning and memory
- Context-dependent attentional guidance
- Statistical regularities in visual environment
- Incidental encoding of spatial configurations
- Interaction between attention and long-term memory
- Implicit vs. explicit knowledge of spatial regularities

**Key References:**
1. Chun, M. M., & Jiang, Y. (1998). Contextual cueing: Implicit learning and memory of visual context guides spatial attention. *Cognitive Psychology*, 36(1), 28–71.
2. Chun, M. M., & Jiang, Y. (2003). Implicit, long-term spatial contextual memory. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition*, 29(2), 224–234.
3. Goujon, A., Didierjean, A., & Thorpe, S. (2015). Investigating implicit statistical learning mechanisms through contextual cueing. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 19(9), 524–533.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Vadillo, M. A., Konstantinidis, E., & Shanks, D. R. (2022). Underpowered samples, false negatives, and unconscious learning. *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 29, 307–337.
2. Zinchenko, A., Conci, M., Müller, H. J., & Geyer, T. (2018). Predictive visual search: Role of environmental regularities in the learning of context cues. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 80, 1096–1109.
3. Sisk, C. A., Remington, R. W., & Jiang, Y. V. (2019). Mechanisms of contextual cueing: A tutorial review. *Attention, Perception, & Psychophysics*, 81, 2571–2589.
4. Annac, E., Conci, M., Müller, H. J., & Geyer, T. (2017). Local item density modulates adaptation of learned contextual cues. *Visual Cognition*, 25(1–3), 262–277.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Visual Search CC**: T among L's with repeated vs. novel configurations; the Chun & Jiang (1998) version.
- **Recognition Test for Awareness**: Post-experiment forced-choice recognition of repeated displays to verify implicit nature of learning.
- **Configuration-Based vs. Association-Based CC**: Manipulating whether the entire configuration or just target-distractor associations drive the effect.
- **Semantic Contextual Cueing**: Meaningful scenes rather than letter arrays; contextual regularities in naturalistic visual environments.
- **Contextual Cueing with Eye Tracking**: Tracking fixation patterns to determine how learned configurations guide overt attention.
- **Cross-Modal Contextual Cueing**: Auditory or tactile context associated with visual target locations.
- **Contextual Cueing in Aging**: Testing whether implicit spatial learning is preserved in healthy aging.
- **Contextual Cueing with Working Memory Load**: Dual-task manipulation to test whether CC depends on working memory resources.
- **Dynamic Contextual Cueing**: Displays with moving elements; tests whether temporal-spatial regularities can be learned.
- **Transfer of Contextual Cueing**: Changing distractor identities or adding/removing distractors to test what aspects of context are learned.

## 5.6 Social Cognition and Theory of Mind

---

### Task 91: False Belief Task (Theory of Mind)

In the canonical version, a participant observes a scenario in which a character (e.g., "Sally") places an object in Location A and then leaves. In her absence, another character moves the object to Location B. The participant is asked where Sally will look for the object. Correctly predicting that Sally will look in Location A requires understanding that Sally holds a false belief about the object's location — a belief that differs from the participant's own knowledge of reality. Wimmer and Perner (1983) introduced this task as the gold-standard test for theory of mind (ToM), the ability to attribute mental states to others. It has become foundational in developmental psychology, social neuroscience, and clinical research on autism, schizophrenia, and other conditions involving social cognitive deficits.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Theory of mind and mental state attribution
- False belief understanding and belief-reality distinction
- Perspective-taking and cognitive empathy
- Executive function demands in belief reasoning
- Social prediction based on others' knowledge states
- Dissociation between own knowledge and others' beliefs

**Key References:**
1. Wimmer, H., & Perner, J. (1983). Beliefs about beliefs: Representation and constraining function of wrong beliefs in young children's understanding of deception. *Cognition*, 13(1), 103–128.
2. Baron-Cohen, S., Leslie, A. M., & Frith, U. (1985). Does the autistic child have a "theory of mind"? *Cognition*, 21(1), 37–46.
3. Saxe, R., & Kanwisher, N. (2003). People thinking about thinking people: The role of the temporo-parietal junction in "theory of mind." *NeuroImage*, 19(4), 1835–1842.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Schurz, M., Radua, J., Aichhorn, M., Richlan, F., & Perner, J. (2014). Fractionating theory of mind: A meta-analysis of functional brain imaging studies. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 42, 9–34.
2. Wellman, H. M., Cross, D., & Watson, J. (2001). Meta-analysis of theory-of-mind development: The truth about false belief. *Child Development*, 72(3), 655–684.
3. Apperly, I. A., & Butterfill, S. A. (2009). Do humans have two systems to track beliefs and belief-like states? *Psychological Review*, 116(4), 953–970.
4. Kulke, L., & Rakoczy, H. (2018). Implicit Theory of Mind: An overview of current replications and non-replications. *Data in Brief*, 16, 101–104.

**Task Variations:**
- **Sally-Anne Task (Baron-Cohen et al., 1985)**: Acted-out version with dolls/puppets; the standard developmental version for young children.
- **Unexpected Transfer / Change of Location**: Object moved while agent is absent; the canonical format for children.
- **Unexpected Contents (Smarties Task)**: Container with unexpected contents; child asked what another person will think is inside.
- **Second-Order False Belief**: "Where does A think that B thinks the object is?"; tests more complex recursive mentalizing.
- **Adult False Belief with Reaction Time**: Adapted for adults using stories or videos with reaction-time dependent measures.
- **Implicit False Belief (Anticipatory Looking)**: Eye-tracking measure in infants; looking anticipation to the belief-consistent location.
- **False Belief with fMRI**: Neuroimaging to identify TPJ and mPFC activation during belief reasoning.
- **Non-Verbal False Belief (Animations)**: Animated shapes displaying belief-consistent behavior without language (Heider & Simmel style).
- **False Belief in Autism Spectrum**: Clinical application; typically impaired in autism, basis for ToM deficit theories.
- **Competitive False Belief (Deception)**: Participant must deceive another agent by exploiting their false belief; tests active use of ToM.

## 5.7 Attention and Dual-Task Performance

---

### Task 92: Sustained Attention to Response Task (SART)

Participants view a series of single digits (1–9) presented one at a time and press a button for every digit except a designated target (typically "3"). Because targets are rare (1 in 9 trials), the task encourages an automatic, rhythmic response pattern, and failures to withhold the response to the target digit are scored as errors of commission — lapses of sustained attention. Robertson et al. (1997) designed the SART as a clinically sensitive measure of everyday attentional failures, finding strong correlations between SART errors and self-reported attentional lapses in both brain-injured patients and healthy controls. The paradigm is now widely used in research on mind-wandering, vigilance, ADHD, traumatic brain injury, and the default mode network.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Sustained attention and vigilance maintenance
- Response inhibition under monotonous conditions
- Mind-wandering and attentional lapses
- Speed-accuracy tradeoff in sustained performance
- Automatic vs. controlled processing
- Self-monitoring and error awareness

**Key References:**
1. Robertson, I. H., Manly, T., Andrade, J., Baddeley, B. T., & Yiend, J. (1997). 'Oops!': Performance correlates of everyday attentional failures in traumatic brain injured and normal subjects. *Neuropsychologia*, 35(6), 747–758.
2. Manly, T., Robertson, I. H., Galloway, M., & Hawkins, K. (1999). The absent mind: Further investigations of sustained attention to response. *Neuropsychologia*, 37(6), 661–670.
3. Smallwood, J., & Schooler, J. W. (2006). The restless mind. *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(6), 946–958.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Seli, P., Cheyne, J. A., & Smilek, D. (2013). Wandering minds and wavering rhythms: Linking mind wandering and behavioral variability. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 39(1), 1–5.
2. Dockree, P. M., Kelly, S. P., Foxe, J. J., Reilly, R. B., & Robertson, I. H. (2007). Optimal sustained attention is linked to the spectral content of background EEG activity. *European Journal of Neuroscience*, 25(3), 900–907.
3. Seli, P., Risko, E. F., Smilek, D., & Schacter, D. L. (2016). Mind-wandering with and without intention. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 20(8), 605–617.
4. Fortenbaugh, F. C., DeGutis, J., & Esterman, M. (2017). Recent theoretical, neural, and clinical advances in sustained attention research. *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 1396(1), 70–91.

**Task Variations:**
- **Fixed SART (Standard)**: Digits 1–9 in ascending, repeating sequence; target = 3.
- **Random SART**: Digits presented in random order; eliminates prediction from sequential position.
- **SART with Thought Probes**: Intermittent probes asking participants whether they were on-task or mind-wandering; links errors to self-reported attentional states.
- **SART with Multiple Targets**: Two or more target digits to vary no-go probability.
- **SART with Variable ISI**: Varying inter-stimulus intervals to manipulate the buildup of automatic responding.
- **SART with EEG**: Measuring pre-error EEG signatures (alpha power, P300 amplitude) to identify neural predictors of lapses.
- **Sustained Attention with Clock Task**: Mackworth Clock Test variant; watching a clock hand for occasional double jumps over extended periods.
- **SART in TBI/ADHD Populations**: Clinical application for quantifying sustained attention deficits.
- **Child-Adapted SART**: Pictorial stimuli (animals, objects) replacing digits for developmental populations.
- **SART with Motivational Manipulation**: Adding feedback, rewards, or punishment for errors; tests whether lapses reflect strategic disengagement or genuine capacity limitation.

### Task 93: Psychological Refractory Period (PRP) Paradigm

Two stimuli are presented in rapid succession (S1 then S2, separated by a variable stimulus onset asynchrony or SOA), each requiring its own speeded response (R1 and R2). The robust finding is that R2 is slowed as the SOA decreases — the psychological refractory period effect — because central response-selection processes for S2 must wait until response selection for S1 is complete. Pashler (1994) provided the definitive review establishing the central bottleneck model: perceptual processing and motor execution can overlap between tasks, but response selection (choosing which response to make) represents a serial bottleneck. The PRP paradigm remains the primary tool for mapping the architecture of cognitive processing stages and understanding the limits of multitasking.

**Cognitive Concepts Tested:**
- Central processing bottleneck and response selection
- Dual-task interference and multitasking limits
- Processing stage architecture (perception, selection, execution)
- Automaticity and practice effects on dual-task performance
- Cognitive slack and absorption of processing time
- Parallel vs. serial processing capacity

**Key References:**
1. Pashler, H. (1994). Dual-task interference in simple tasks: Data and theory. *Psychological Bulletin*, 116(2), 220–244.
2. Telford, C. W. (1931). The refractory phase of voluntary and associative responses. *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 14(1), 1–36.
3. Pashler, H., & Johnston, J. C. (1998). Attentional limitations in dual-task performance. In H. Pashler (Ed.), *Attention* (pp. 155–189). Psychology Press.

**Additional Recent References:**
1. Tombu, M., & Jolicoeur, P. (2003). A central capacity sharing model of dual-task performance. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 29(1), 3–18.
2. Sigman, M., & Dehaene, S. (2008). Brain mechanisms of serial and parallel processing during dual-task performance. *Journal of Neuroscience*, 28(30), 7585–7598.
3. Strobach, T., Schubert, T., Pashler, H., & Rickard, T. (2014). The specificity of stimulus-response and response-response compatibility effects in dual tasks. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance*, 40(5), 1966–1984.
4. Zickerick, B., Thönes, S., Kobald, S. O., Wascher, E., Schneider, D., & Küper, K. (2021). Differential effects of the psychological refractory period on early perceptual processing. *Psychophysiology*, 58(5), e13791.

**Task Variations:**
- **Standard Auditory-Visual PRP**: S1 = tone (classify pitch), S2 = letter/digit (classify identity); the canonical design.
- **Visual-Visual PRP**: Both stimuli are visual; requires spatial separation to avoid confusion.
- **Variable SOA Design**: SOAs ranging from 50 to 1000 ms to trace the full PRP function.
- **Locus-of-Slack Logic**: Manipulating S2 difficulty at perceptual vs. response-selection stages; if perceptual difficulty is absorbed into slack at short SOAs, the bottleneck is post-perceptual.
- **PRP with Backward Compatibility Effect**: Response features of S2 influence R1 at short SOAs; reveals parallel activation of response codes.
- **PRP with Practice**: Extensive training to test whether the bottleneck can be eliminated or reduced.
- **PRP with Ideomotor-Compatible Tasks**: Tasks with strong S-R compatibility (e.g., say the word you see) to test whether compatible tasks bypass the bottleneck.
- **PRP with EEG**: P300 latency tracks the postponement of S2 central processing; lateralized readiness potential (LRP) isolates response activation timing.
- **PRP in Aging**: Testing whether older adults show larger PRP effects consistent with slowed central processing.
- **Triple-Task PRP**: Three overlapping tasks to further constrain models of central processing capacity.
